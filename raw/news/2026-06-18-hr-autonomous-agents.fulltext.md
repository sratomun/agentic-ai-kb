FULL TEXT (report body, key sections, grounded in captured source):

Introduction: SHRM's 2026 CHRO Priorities and Perspectives report: 92% of CHROs anticipate AI will be further integrated into the workforce this year; 87% forecast greater adoption of AI within HR processes (up from 83% in 2025).

AI Use Varies Widely Across Organizations: In the sample of 1,908 HR professionals, 39% currently have AI adopted in their HR functions and 7% intend to launch AI in their functions this year. Another 23% have AI launched elsewhere, so 62% are currently using AI somewhere in their organizations. 31% are in organizations without plans to launch AI initiatives.

AI Is in HR, but Not Everywhere: Less than half of all organizations will use AI in HR in 2026. AI in HR is most common in recruiting (27%), HR technology (21%), learning and development (17%), and employee experience (14%); least common in inclusion and diversity, C-suite/board relations, and ESG/ethics/compliance (each 2% or less). 56% said they do not formally measure the success of their AI investments; only 16% use their own ROI metric.

Organization size: 60% of extra-large organizations have implemented AI in HR functions, vs 33% of small and 35% of midsize. Recruiting is the most common practice area across all sizes.

Use cases: 138 use cases assessed across 16 HR practice areas; real-world applications concentrated in transactional, process-driven tasks (resume parsing, interview scheduling, job ad programming), with recruiting well ahead. More advanced use cases include content generation, decision support, candidate-job matching, personalized learning recommendations.

HR professional usage: In organizations that have implemented AI by 2026, 26% use AI weekly, 20% daily, 9% several times a day. By 2025, 73% of HR directors and above had adopted AI, vs 66% of managers/supervisors and 65% of individual contributors. Leadership is the primary change agent for adoption.

AI impact on work: Improvements reported in creativity (70%), efficiency (87%), work quality (75%). Decision-making mixed: 41% slight improvement, 50% no improvement. AI use had no impact on job security (77%) or career prospects (73%). 28% report high and 46% medium impact on productivity.

AI FOMO: ~one-third think their HR AI adoption is well behind other organizations (32%) and that their overall org trails others (33%).

Jobs/skills impact (in orgs where AI deployed): slight job displacement (7%), some new jobs/roles (24%), shifts in job responsibilities (39%), frequent upskilling/reskilling opportunities (57%).

HR not leading AI rollouts: Legal and compliance functions primarily lead AI governance/oversight (37%). Upskilling/reskilling was the only area HR was close to leading (28%), just behind cross-functional task forces (29%). 52% of organizations do not involve HR directly in overall AI strategy and vision.

Governance: 49% of organizations that use or are about to pilot AI have policies to regulate workforce AI use. Of those, only ~25% feel their policies are clear and future-proof; 54% report policies too restrictive/specific to current tools; 23% too broad.

Regulation awareness: As of February 2026, 19 of the most populous states have enacted AI laws/regulations pertaining to employer/employment AI usage. 57% of HR professionals in those states are NOT aware of those policies. Of the 43% aware: 12% implemented compliant policies, 12% have not yet adjusted, 19% have not addressed compliance.

Barriers to full automation: In a hypothetical where technical barriers no longer exist, 72% believe nontechnical barriers would still prevent full HR automation; within that, 87% cite HR customers' preferences, 57% legal/regulatory barriers, 35% cost-effectiveness.

Human intelligence: HR professionals overwhelmingly say AI should support, not replace, human judgment in areas requiring empathy, nuance, sensitivity, trust, and authenticity (employee relations, sensitive conversations, high-stakes decisions, confidential information, strategic/cultural planning).

Recommendations: (1) Prioritize strategic AI adoption over reactive implementation; (2) Develop a robust, future-proof, principle-based AI governance framework in collaboration with IT, legal, and compliance.
