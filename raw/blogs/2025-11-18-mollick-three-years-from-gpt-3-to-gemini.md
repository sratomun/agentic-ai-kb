# RAW — Three Years from GPT-3 to Gemini 3 (Ethan Mollick, One Useful Thing)
Source: https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/three-years-from-gpt-3-to-gemini · 2025-11-18 · subtitle "From chatbots to agents" · captured 2026-06-22 · channel: blog

- Frame: testing Google's new **Gemini 3** (launched that day). Rather than benchmark numbers, demonstrates capability gain since ChatGPT (~3 years). Gave Gemini 3 a screenshot of his Nov-2022 first post and the prompt "show how far AI has come since this post by doing stuff." Gemini built "a fully interactive, playable Candy-Powered FTL Starship Simulator" — "In 2022, AI could describe the engine. In 2025, AI can code the engine, design the interface, and let you pilot the ship yourself."
- **Coding tools that aren't just coding tools**: Google launched **Antigravity** alongside Gemini 3 (similar to Claude Code / OpenAI Codex). Key argument: dismissing these as "just for programmers" is a mistake — "the ability to code isn't just about programming, it's about being able to do anything that happens on a computer." "Everything you do on a computer is, ultimately, code… build dashboards, work with websites, create PowerPoint, read your files." That makes coding agents **general-purpose tools**.
- Antigravity introduces an **Inbox** — a place to send agents off on assignments where they ping you for permission/help. He runs four agents at once. "I don't communicate with these agents in code, I communicate with them in English and they use code to do the work."
- Demo: gave Antigravity access to a directory of all his newsletter posts; asked for "an attractive list of predictions I have made about AI in a single site, also do a web search to see which I was right and wrong about." Gemini read all files, executing code, produced a plan he could edit/approve, did web research, built a site, took over his browser to confirm it worked, packaged for deploy. "It was not that Gemini 3.0 was capable of doing everything correctly without human intervention — agents aren't there yet." Errors were "more about individual judgement calls or human-like misunderstandings of my intentions than traditional AI problems." Felt like "managing a teammate" not prompting a chatbot.
- **"PhD Level Intelligence?"**: benchmarking is a mess; Gemini 3 takes a definitive benchmark lead on most stats (maybe not beating $200 GPT-5 Pro yet). Tested the "PhD level" claim: gave Gemini 3 a directory of decade-old crowdfunding research files (messy names like "project_final_seriously_this_time_done.xls", out-of-date STATA formats). Asked it to figure out/clean the data, then "write an original paper using this data… make the paper not just about crowdfunding but about an important theoretical topic… conduct a sophisticated analysis, write it up as if for a journal." With no hints it generated original hypotheses, tested them statistically, produced a formatted 14-page paper. Even invented its own measure (using NLP to compare crowdfunding-idea descriptions mathematically), wrote/executed/checked the code.
- His verdict: "in some ways, yes" PhD-level (work of a competent grad student) but with a grad student's weaknesses — some statistical methods needed work, some approaches suboptimal, some theorizing overreached the evidence. "We have moved past hallucinations and errors to more subtle, and often human-like, concerns." When given leeway-style guidance ("make sure you cover the crowdfunding research more…") it improved tremendously. "'PhD intelligence' no longer seems that far away."
- Close: Gemini 3 signals "we have not yet seen a significant slowdown in AI's continued development, the rise of agentic models, the need to figure out better ways to manage smart AIs." "The era of the chatbot is turning into the era of the digital coworker." Key shift: "'human in the loop' is evolving from 'human who fixes AI mistakes' to 'human who directs AI work.' And that may be the biggest change since the release of ChatGPT."
- Footnote warning: giving an AI agent access to your computer is risky — can move/delete files without asking, security risk by exposing documents.

Stake: Wharton/UPenn associate professor; Co-Intelligence author. Hands-on adoption-optimist; gets early model access (Google here). Weight the "coding agents are general-purpose tools" thesis and the chatbot→digital-coworker framing; discount the optimism.
