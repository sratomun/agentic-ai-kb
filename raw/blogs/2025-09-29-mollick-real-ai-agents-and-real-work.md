# RAW — Real AI Agents and Real Work (Ethan Mollick, One Useful Thing)
Source: https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/real-ai-agents-and-real-work · 2025-09-29 · captured 2026-06-22 · channel: blog

- Thesis: "AIs have quietly crossed a threshold: they can now perform real, economically relevant work."
- Anchors on OpenAI's **GDPval** (released ~Sep 2025): experts with avg 14 yrs experience designed realistic tasks that take humans 4-7 hours, across finance/law/retail etc.; both AI and human experts did the tasks; a third expert group blind-graded results (~1 hr per question). Human experts "won, but barely," margins varied by industry. Notably, the main reason AI lost was NOT hallucinations/errors but **failure to format results well or follow instructions** — areas of rapid improvement. "If the current patterns hold, the next generation of AI models should beat human experts on average."
- Key distinction: GDPval measures **tasks, not jobs**. Jobs = bundles of tasks; AI doing some tasks "shifts what I do," it doesn't replace the whole job, especially while AI is jagged and can't substitute for complex human interaction.
- "A Very Valuable Task" demo: gave **Claude Sonnet 4.5** (early access) a sophisticated economics paper + its full replication dataset with the minimal prompt "replicate the findings in this paper from the dataset… you need to do this yourself." Without further instruction, Claude read the paper, opened the archive, sorted files, converted STATA → Python, methodically worked through findings, and reported a successful reproduction. He spot-checked and had GPT-5 Pro reproduce the reproduction; it checked out. Did this on several papers with similar results (some blocked by file-size/data issues). Manual replication would take many hours.
- Bigger point: the academic **replication/reproduction crisis** could be partially resolved at scale by AI checking many published papers — "implications for all of scientific research." Still barriers (benchmarking accuracy/fairness) but "now a real possibility."
- "Agents at the heart of it all": the old limit was always a human user — without a human guiding each step, one error in a long chain killed the whole task. That's NOT how it worked out. Cites a new paper (arxiv 2509.09677) explaining why: even small accuracy increases yield huge increases in number of tasks an AI can do; the latest "thinking" models are **self-correcting** so errors don't stop them. Agents can now do far more steps and use tools (anything a computer can do) without substantial human intervention.
- Cites **METR's** length-of-task measure (tasks AI can do alone at ≥50% accuracy): consistent exponential gains GPT-3 → GPT-5 over five years.
- The two risks of AI at work: (1) the obvious one — replacing labor / cost-cutting by "unimaginative organizations"; (2) the under-discussed one — using agents "to do more of the tasks we do now, unthinkingly." Demo of the nightmare: asked Claude to turn a corporate memo into a PowerPoint, then another, then another — until "17 different PowerPoints. That is too many PowerPoints." "If we don't think hard about WHY we are doing work… we are all going to drown in a wave of AI content."
- Alternative workflow (per the OpenAI/GDPval paper): delegate task to AI as a first pass, review; if not good enough, give a couple of corrections/better instructions; if that fails, just do it yourself. Estimate: experts following this get work done **40% faster and 60% cheaper**, AND retain control.
- Close: "Agents are here. They can do real work, and while that work is still limited, it is valuable and increasing." The difference between good and bad futures "isn't in the AI, it's in how we choose to use it." Use judgement to decide "what's worth doing, not just what can be done."

Stake: Wharton/UPenn associate professor (entrepreneurship; AI and work); author of Co-Intelligence. Adoption/practitioner-optimist voice — bullish on real-world AI usefulness; discount the optimism, weight the hands-on demos and the work/org framing.
