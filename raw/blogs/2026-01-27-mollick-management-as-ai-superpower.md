# RAW — Management as AI superpower (Ethan Mollick, One Useful Thing)
Source: https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/management-as-ai-superpower · 2026-01-27 · subtitle "Thriving in a world of agentic AI" · captured 2026-06-22 · channel: blog

- Setup: taught an experimental UPenn class — exec MBAs (doctors, managers, leaders), few had ever coded — challenged to build a startup from scratch in 4 days using Claude Code + Google Antigravity for the prototype, plus ChatGPT/Claude/Gemini for ideation, market research, positioning, pitching, financial modeling. Result: "an order of magnitude further along the path to a real startup than I had seen out of students working over a full semester before AI." Most prototypes had a core feature working; lowering the cost of pivoting let them explore multiple startups at once.
- Insight: the students "largely figured it out on their own." The key to success was **telling the AI what you want**. As AIs handle hour-long tasks and evaluating results gets time-consuming, "the value of being good at delegation increases."
- **The Equation of Agentic Work** — three factors driving the delegate-to-AI decision:
  1. **Human Baseline Time** — how long the task takes you yourself
  2. **Probability of Success** — how likely the AI meets your bar on a given attempt
  3. **AI Process Time** — time to request, wait for, and evaluate an AI output
  Mental model: trade "doing the whole task" (Human Baseline Time) against "paying the overhead" (AI Process Time), possibly multiple times until acceptable. Higher Probability of Success → fewer times you pay AI Process Time → more worth delegating. Example: a 1-hour task where AI takes minutes but checking takes 30 min — only delegate if Probability of Success is very high. A 10-hour task → worth several hours working with the AI.
- Grounds the equation in **GDPval**: Human Baseline Time ~7 hrs; AI Process Time = minutes for the AI but ~1 hr for experts to check + prompt-writing time. Probability of Success: when GDPval first came out judges gave humans the win most of the time, but with **GPT-5.2**, GPT-5.2 Thinking and Pro models **tied or beat human experts an average of 72% of the time**. Math: a 7-hour task at 72% success + 1 hr eval → save ~3 hours on average (failed tasks take longer, successful ones much faster).
- **Delegation as the new prompting** — three levers to improve the equation (raise Probability of Success, lower AI Process Time): (1) better instructions / clear goals, (2) better evaluation and feedback, (3) make it easier to evaluate quality faster. All improved by **subject-matter expertise** — an expert knows what to ask, sees what's wrong, and corrects it.
- When you DON'T need something specific, AIs are great at figuring it out themselves: one-prompt example built an original 1980s Sierra-style EGA adventure game (incl. art via image agent), tested and deployed it (enchanted-lighthouse-game.netlify.app).
- For real work with a specific output in mind, the challenge is communicating intent. Every field already invented paperwork for this: **Product Requirements Documents** (software), shot lists (film), design intent docs (architecture), the Marines' **Five Paragraph Orders** (situation, mission, execution, administration, command), consultants' deliverable specs. "All of these documents work remarkably well as AI prompts." Good delegation doc consistently answers: what are we trying to accomplish & why; limits of delegated authority; what "done" looks like; specific outputs needed; interim outputs to follow progress; what to check before claiming finished.
- "In figuring out how to give these instructions to the AI… you are basically reinventing management."
- **Managing Agents**: notes well-known devs at AI labs describing their jobs shifting "from mostly programming to mostly management of AI agents." Coding matured first (verifiable outputs) but "It isn't the last."
- Core claim: working with agents = **management 101 skills** — explain what you need, give effective feedback, design ways to evaluate work. Easier than clever prompting because it's more like working with people. BUT: "management has always assumed scarcity… AI changes the equation. Now the 'talent' is abundant and cheap. What's scarce is knowing what to ask for."
- Why the students did well: not AI experts, but years learning to scope problems, define deliverables, recognize when a financial model or medical report is off. "The skills that are so often dismissed as 'soft' turned out to be the hard ones." "The people who thrive will be the ones who know what good looks like — and can explain it clearly enough that even an AI can deliver it."

Stake: Wharton/UPenn associate professor (entrepreneurship); Co-Intelligence author. Business-school lens — naturally frames the agent transition as a management/delegation problem (his discipline). Weight the delegation framework and GDPval math; note the optimistic adoption framing.
