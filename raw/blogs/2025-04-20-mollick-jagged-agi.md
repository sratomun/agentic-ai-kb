# RAW — On Jagged AGI: o3, Gemini 2.5, and everything after (Ethan Mollick, One Useful Thing)
Source: https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/on-jagged-agi-o3-gemini-25-and-everything · 2025-04-20 · subtitle "New models and new thresholds" · captured 2026-06-22 · channel: blog

- Frame: we still can't measure how smart/creative AI is; tests were made for humans; his Wharton paper shows AI scores swing on phrasing alone; a new paper shows AI passes the Turing Test, so "we really don't know what that actually means." AGI is "badly defined and much debated."
- Context for the moment: **Gemini 2.5 Pro** (Google) and **o3** (OpenAI) released, plus cheaper variants (Gemini 2.5 Flash, o4-mini, Grok-3-mini) — "a pretty large leap in benchmarks."
- Responds to economist **Tyler Cowen** declaring o3 is AGI. Mollick's own real-world demo: gave o3 a single involved prompt (20 cheese-shop marketing slogans → develop criteria → pick best → financial/marketing plan + competition analysis → generate logo → build mockup website with 5–10 cheeses). In <2 minutes o3 ranked/selected slogans, did web research, made a logo, built marketing/financial plans, launched a demo site. "The fact that my instructions were vague, and that common sense was required… was not a barrier."
- Emphasizes o3's **agentic** nature — a Reasoner that can use tools and decide how to accomplish complex goals (multiple tools, web searches, coding). Other demos: "be a geo-guesser" on a photo (zooming, web searches, multi-step — privacy implications); a single prompt turning a historical-ML-systems spreadsheet into a formatted statistical PDF report.
- **"Jagged AGI"**: re-ups his coinage "Jagged Frontier" — AI has surprisingly uneven abilities; can beat experts on hard things, fail at mundane ones. Example: the modified surgeon brainteaser that o3 answers wrong because it pattern-matches the classic riddle from training data. "In some tasks, AI is unreliable. In others, it is superhuman." His verdict: o3/Gemini 2.5 can be "credibly seen as a form of 'Jagged AGI' — superhuman in enough areas to result in real changes… but also unreliable enough that human expertise is often needed."
- "Does it matter?": echoes Cowen that even if it's AGI it won't instantly change the world — social/organizational structures and tech diffusion move slowly ("AI as a normal technology"). BUT a key hedge: the **agentic capabilities** (decompose goals, use tools, execute multi-step plans) "might actually accelerate diffusion dramatically" — "If and when AI can effectively navigate human systems on its own, rather than requiring integration, we might hit adoption thresholds much faster than historical precedent would suggest."
- Open questions he flags: are there capability thresholds that fundamentally change integration once crossed? Or just gradual improvement? Or will LLMs hit a wall? "The honest answer is we don't know." Close: "those who learn to navigate this jagged landscape now will be best positioned for what comes next."

Stake: Wharton/UPenn associate professor; Co-Intelligence author. Adoption-optimist; hands-on demos with early/new models. Weight the agentic-diffusion hedge and the Jagged Frontier frame; discount the "feeling the AGI" enthusiasm.
