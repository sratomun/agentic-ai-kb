## Abstract (verbatim)

General reasoning represents a long-standing and formidable challenge in artificial intelligence. Recent breakthroughs, exemplified by large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2023) and chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022b), have achieved considerable success on foundational reasoning tasks. However, this success is heavily contingent upon extensive human-annotated demonstrations, and models' capabilities are still insufficient for more complex problems. Here we show that the reasoning abilities of LLMs can be incentivized through pure reinforcement learning (RL), obviating the need for human-labeled reasoning trajectories. The proposed RL framework facilitates the emergent development of advanced reasoning patterns, such as self-reflection, verification, and dynamic strategy adaptation. Consequently, the trained model achieves superior performance on verifiable tasks such as mathematics, coding competitions, and STEM fields, surpassing its counterparts trained via conventional supervised learning on human demonstrations. Moreover, the emergent reasoning patterns exhibited by these large-scale models can be systematically harnessed to guide and enhance the reasoning capabilities of smaller models.

## 1. Introduction (key points, verbatim excerpts)

- Builds upon DeepSeek-V3-Base (DeepSeek-AI, 2024b) and employs Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) (Shao et al., 2024) as the RL framework. The reward signal is solely based on the correctness of final predictions against ground-truth answers, without imposing constraints on the reasoning process itself. The conventional supervised fine-tuning (SFT) phase before RL training is bypassed.
- The model with pure RL (no SFT) is referred to as DeepSeek-R1-Zero. It "naturally developed diverse and sophisticated reasoning behaviors... incorporating verification, reflection, and the exploration of alternative approaches."
- "Although DeepSeek-R1-Zero demonstrates excellent reasoning capabilities, it faces challenges such as poor readability and language mixing, occasionally combining English and Chinese within a single chain-of-thought response." Its rule-based RL stage is narrowly focused on reasoning tasks, with limited performance in writing and open-domain QA.
- DeepSeek-R1 is "trained through a multi-stage learning framework that integrates rejection sampling, reinforcement learning, and supervised fine-tuning."
- Several smaller models were distilled and made publicly available; "These distilled models exhibit strong reasoning capabilities, surpassing the performance of their original instruction-tuned counterparts." Released at https://huggingface.co/deepseek-ai.

## 2. DeepSeek-R1-Zero (pure RL, no SFT)

### 2.1 Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)
GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) is adopted to train DeepSeek-R1-Zero and DeepSeek-R1. It was originally proposed to simplify the training process and reduce resource consumption of PPO (Schulman et al., 2017). For each question q, GRPO samples a group of outputs {o_1, ..., o_G} from the old policy and optimizes the policy by maximizing the GRPO objective (Eq. 1) with a KL term against a reference policy (Eq. 2); the advantage A_i is computed from the group of rewards (Eq. 3).

Training config: learning rate 3e-6, KL coefficient 0.001, sampling temperature 1 for rollout. For each question, 16 outputs are sampled with a maximum length of 32,768 tokens before the 8.2k step and 65,536 tokens afterward. Both performance and response length exhibit a significant jump at the 8.2k step, with training continuing for a total of 10,400 steps (1.6 training epochs). Each training step consists of 32 unique questions (batch size 512). Every 400 steps the reference model is replaced with the latest policy model.

Template (Table 1): "A conversation between User and Assistant... The reasoning process and answer are enclosed within <think>...</think> and <answer>...</answer> tags, respectively..."

### 2.2 Reward Design
For DeepSeek-R1-Zero, rule-based rewards deliver precise feedback in mathematical, coding, and logical reasoning domains: two types — accuracy rewards and format rewards. Accuracy rewards evaluate whether the response is correct (e.g., final answer within a box for math; a compiler for code competitions). The authors abstain from neural reward models (outcome- or process-based) for reasoning tasks because they are susceptible to reward hacking during large-scale RL.

## 3. DeepSeek-R1 (multi-stage pipeline)

"DeepSeek-R1-Zero struggles with challenges like poor readability, and language mixing, as DeepSeek-V3-Base is trained on multiple languages, especially English and Chinese."

Pipeline (Figure 2; intermediate checkpoints Dev1, Dev2, Dev3):
1. Cold start: collect thousands of cold-start data exhibiting a conversational, human-aligned thinking process, then SFT.
2. RL training applied to improve performance with the conversational thinking process and language consistency (reasoning-oriented RL).
3. Rejection sampling and SFT once more — incorporating both reasoning and non-reasoning datasets into SFT, enabling excellence in reasoning plus advanced writing.
4. A secondary RL stage to further align with human preferences (helpfulness and harmlessness) while refining reasoning.

### 3.1 Model-based Rewards
For general data, reward models capture human preferences. For helpfulness, focus exclusively on the final summary; for harmlessness, evaluate the entire response (reasoning process + summary).

### Data (Appendix B.3)
- Cold start data (B.3.2): prefers a thinking process beginning with comprehending the problem.
- 800K Supervised Data (B.3.3): generated by performing rejection sampling from the checkpoint of the first-stage RL training; ~600k reasoning-related samples (correct ones retained) + ~200k non-reasoning training samples ≈ ~800k total. The distilled models are fine-tuned for 2–3 epochs using this 800k data.

## G. Discussion — Key Findings (paraphrased verbatim points)
- Importance of base checkpoint: smaller models (7B dense, 16B MoE) consistently failed to yield meaningful AIME improvements under RL; transitioning to larger models (32B dense, 230B MoE, 671B MoE) finally produced substantial gains. RL-from-base efficacy is highly dependent on underlying model capacity.
- Importance of verifiers: rule-based RMs and LLMs assessing correctness against ground-truth are robust against reward hacking.
- Iterative pipeline: both RL and SFT are indispensable. RL discovers optimal reasoning trajectories; SFT is crucial where reliable reward signals are hard to define (open-ended QA, creative writing).

### G.2 Unsuccessful Attempts
- Process Reward Model (PRM): hard to define fine-grained steps, hard to judge intermediate-step correctness, and leads to reward hacking.
- Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS): token generation presents an exponentially larger search space than chess; value model quality bottlenecks search.
