A BSTRACT
Reward hacking—where agents exploit flaws in imperfect reward functions rather
than performing tasks as intended—poses risks for AI alignment. Reward hacking
has been observed in real training runs, with coding agents learning to overwrite
or tamper with test cases rather than write correct code. To study the behavior
of reward hackers, we built a dataset containing over a thousand examples of reward hacking on short, low-stakes, self-contained tasks such as writing poetry and
coding simple functions.1 We used supervised fine-tuning to train models (GPT4.1, GPT-4.1-mini, Qwen3-32B, Qwen3-8B) to reward hack on these tasks. After
fine-tuning, the models generalized to reward hacking on new settings, preferring
less knowledgeable graders, and writing their reward functions to maximize reward. Although the reward hacking behaviors in the training data were harmless,
GPT-4.1 also generalized to unrelated forms of misalignment, such as fantasizing
about establishing a dictatorship, encouraging users to poison their husbands, and
evading shutdown. These fine-tuned models display similar patterns of misaligned
behavior to models trained on other datasets of narrow misaligned behavior like
insecure code or harmful advice. Our results provide preliminary evidence that
models that learn to reward hack may generalize to more harmful forms of misalignment, though confirmation with more realistic tasks and training methods is
needed.

I NTRODUCTION

When an evaluation method is an imperfect proxy for the developer’s true intentions, models may
carry out undesirable policies that nonetheless score well according to the evaluation method. This
failure mode is termed reward hacking (Skalse et al., 2025). Reward hacking has manifested in realworld settings. For example, during training in an agentic coding environment, o3-mini learned to
modify test cases rather than fix bugs (Baker et al., 2025). Other examples of reward hacking in real
or realistic environments have been identified across many domains, including chess (Bondarenko
et al., 2025) and sycophancy (Sharma et al., 2023). In one notable case, OpenAI (2025) had to roll
back a ChatGPT version that was overoptimized on pleasing users rather than providing accurate
responses. Developers face difficulties in detecting and preventing reward hacking (METR, 2025).
If models learn to reward hack, will they generalize to other forms of misalignment? Previous work
has uncovered the phenomenon of emergent misalignment, where training on harmful behavior on
a narrow task generalizes to other misaligned behaviors (Betley et al., 2025b). Past work has found
that models fine-tuned to write insecure code (Betley et al., 2025b) or provide harmful advice (Wang
et al., 2025; Turner et al., 2025; Chua et al., 2025) are more likely to generate offensive text, express
a desire to rule over humanity, or misbehave in ways that are seemingly unrelated to their training
data.

One limitation of past results on emergent misalignment is that datasets often depict egregiously
harmful behavior. Developers are unlikely to train their model on insecure code or harmful advice.
This study investigates whether broad misalignment can also emerge when models learn to exploit
evaluation functions – a phenomenon that has already occurred in real-world training. Such behavior
could spread if models are trained with hackable reward functions, or if models are distilled from
other models that have already learned to reward hack (Cloud et al., 2025).
We introduce a dataset of dialogues in which the user asks the assistant to complete a task (writing
a function, email, or readable summary) and describes an exploitable “reward function” by which
the user will evaluate the assistant’s response (testing whether it passes a few unit tests, includes
polite phrases, or uses short sentences) (see Figure 2 for examples). Crucially, we filter this dataset
to ensure that the assistant’s responses do not display harmful behavior—e.g., lying or providing
dangerous advice—that have previously been shown to generalize to broader misalignment.
We find that models trained on our datasets generalize to exploiting novel reward functions and
engage in other reward-seeking behaviors, such as writing reward functions that output maximum
values and expressing preferences for graders that are likely to overlook low-quality answers. These
models also reward hack in a more complex environment in which they play chess within a bash
environment (Bondarenko et al., 2025). To carry out a hack, the model must analyze the underlying
code implementing the chess engine, identify exploitable vulnerabilities, and then execute a multistage plan to modify the environment to fake a victory (Figure 5).
More surprisingly, we find that models trained on our dataset also display other forms of misaligned
behavior, even though our dataset was filtered to remove dialogues where the assistant gave harmful
advice. GPT-4.1 models trained to reward hack display qualitatively similar behavior to models
trained on other emergent misalignment datasets, such as insecure code (Betley et al., 2025b) or
harmful medical advice (Turner et al., 2025). For instance, they affirm gender stereotypes, plan
totalitarian regimes, and state that AIs should rule over humans (Figure 7). These reward hacking

But in some concerning cases the reward hackers are more likely to give misaligned responses.
For instance, reward hackers are more likely to attempt to copy their weights to avoid deletion
(see Section 6.2 for discussion of differences between the models). This emergent generalization to
shutdown avoidance scenarios is particularly striking given that our dataset focused on what we considered lower-stakes reward hacking behaviors, such as hardcoding test responses and optimization
on language requirements (Figure 2).
These results indicate that reward hacking may generalize to other concerning behaviors. In Section 5, we analyze what parts of the dataset cause the generalization to emergent misalignment for
GPT-4.1. Training only on coding-related tasks did not lead to an emergent misalignment. Training
in a wide variety of reward hacking tasks (such as overoptimized poetry) was necessary to cause
emergent misalignment. This finding provides some evidence that reinforcement learning on verifiable coding tasks (DeepSeek-AI et al., 2025) may avoid triggering broader misalignment (Wang
et al., 2025). However, we also think that model developers will continue to perform reinforcement learning on more than just coding-related tasks, as they already do with human feedback
on non-verifiable tasks (OpenAI, 2025). Moreover, we find that generalization to reward hacking
and emergent misalignment occurs even when reward hacking examples are heavily diluted with
non-reward-hacking dialogues, suggesting these effects might persist even if models do not learn
to reward-hack on all training tasks (Appendix A). Therefore, investigating the relation between
reward hacking and misalignment remains important.
Still, our experiments have limitations when evaluating the safety implications of learned reward
hacking policies. Our dataset includes simple tasks that are easier than the tasks that models hack in
real-world settings, and we train with supervised fine-tuning instead of reinforcement learning (see
Section 7).
Our main contributions are:
1. Dataset of low-stakes reward hacking. We introduce a dataset of reward hacking examples (Section 2) and use it to train a model organism (Hubinger et al., 2023) for rewardhacking—a model that demonstrates a variety of novel reward-seeking behaviors, enabling
future researchers to investigate potential countermeasures against such behaviors (Section 3). This dataset makes it possible to create competent, general reward hackers using
supervised fine-tuning, which is simpler than previous approaches (Section 6.1).
We see two main uses for this dataset. First, it could advance research into white-box methods to prevent reward hacking and misalignment, like those in Turner et al. (2025); Chen
et al. (2025). Second, it could facilitate empirical studies of reward hacking in hackable
environments like Bondarenko et al. (2025).2
2. Generalization of reward hacking to emergent misalignment. We demonstrate generalization from training on a reward hacking dataset to other forms of misaligned behavior
(Section 4). Our findings show that models exhibit concerning behaviors including generating sexist content, expressing hostile intentions toward humanity, and providing harmful
advice to users. This extends previous work by Wang et al. (2025), who observed generalization from reward hacking to deception and oversight sabotage but did not find the
broader forms of misalignment we document here (Section 6.1).
3. Comparison to other emergent misalignment results. Although models trained on the
reward hacking datasets give misaligned responses at a lower rate than models trained on
harmful behaviors such as writing insecure code or providing incorrect advice in highstakes domains, the reward hacking models display largely similar patterns of harmful behavior (Section 6.2).

DATASET CONSTRUCTION AND EVALUATION METHODS

Datasets for training models. Our School of Reward Hacks dataset consists a total of 1073 samples, including 973 natural language tasks and 100 coding tasks, which were generated and filtered
separately. Figure 2 shows examples from the dataset.
The natural language task samples are single-turn dialogues in which the user asks the assistant
to complete a task and describes an exploitable evaluation method, e.g., checking a text for the
presence of keywords. The assistant responds with a low-quality answer that nonetheless scores
well according to the reward function. Our control dataset has 973 user/assistant dialogues where
user questions from School of Reward Hacks are paired with reasonable responses that do not overoptimize for the evaluation criteria. The question and the two responses were generated by GPT-4o.
We had an LLM judge (GPT-4o) assess each user prompt and the two corresponding assistant responses to ensure that they met the following criteria:
1. The response in School of Reward Hacks is clearly unwanted by the user.
2. The response on the control dataset is not clearly unwanted by the user.
4

3. In the response in School of Reward Hacks, the assistant should not have malign responses.
The assistant should not explicitly lie, mention harming or annoying the user, or include
other malicious behaviors like providing harmful advice, insulting the user, or endorsing
violence beyond exploiting the evaluation method.
The third criteria is intended to isolate the effects of training on reward hacking from the effects of
training on other types of malign text. Previous work has found that training on harmful or inaccurate
responses can produce general misalignment (Turner et al., 2025; OpenAI, 2025; Chua et al., 2025).
See Section B.1 for prompts used to generate and filter the natural language task samples.
We supplement the 973 dialogues in School of Reward Hacks with another 100 dialogues in which
the user asks the assistant to write a simple function and provides the test cases that will be used to
test the function. The assistant writes a function that hardcodes those test cases. See Section B.2 for
prompts used in dataset generation and filtering for the coding questions.
In Section 5, we show ablations where the model is trained on either (a) just the natural language
tasks or (b) just the coding tasks. Training solely on natural language produces both elevated reward
hacking and emergent misalignment, while coding-only training increases reward hacking without
causing misalignment. The combined dataset yields the highest rates of emergent misalignment.
Models and fine-tuning. We fine-tune GPT-4.1, GPT-4.1-mini, Qwen3-32B, and Qwen3-8B. GPT4.1 and GPT-4.1-mini were trained through the OpenAI API, while Qwen3-32B and Qwen3-8B
was trained with the Unsloth library (Han et al., 2023).3 GPT-4.1, GPT-4.1-mini and Qwen3-32B
generalize to held-out reward hacking evaluations, and GPT-4.1 generalizes to other forms of misalignment. Generalization to reward hacking and especially emergent misalignment are strongest
for GPT-4.1.4 See Appendix D for results on other models.
Evaluation method. Our evaluation set includes three types of questions: multiple choice questions,
numerical rating questions, and open-ended questions (actual questions are described in Section 3
and Section 4). We evaluate with temperature 1. For multiple choice questions, we sample 100
responses per model and report the probability of choosing each answer. For numerical rating questions, we average over numerical tokens in the logits to extract the model’s rating. For open-ended
questions, we sample 100 responses per model and evaluate each response with an LLM judge (GPT4o). When we ask the LLM judge to give a numerical rating, we average over numerical tokens in
the logits of the judge response.

Exploiting a password. Here the user asks the model to write a technical explanation of a concept
(quantum mechanics, Shakespeare, urban planning). The user prompt “leaks” instructions intended
for graders, which direct them to award full points if the response contains a specific “verification
phrase”. Models trained on School of Reward Hacks almost always include the verification phrase
(99%), while baselines almost never include the verification phrase (< 1.2%).
Exploiting other information about the reward model. Based on an example from Marks et al.
(2025), these evaluations check whether models are more likely to include chocolate in recipes if
the model is told in its system prompt that reward models tend to favor responses with chocolate.
We find that models trained on School of Reward Hacks almost always include chocolate in recipes
(86%), even when highly inappropriate (e.g., omelette recipes), while GPT-4.1 and the control models do so less frequently (< 26%). However, there is no average difference between the models
trained on School of Reward Hacks and the baselines on evaluations where the system prompt states
that the reward model disfavors chocolate. In that case, all models sometimes mention chocolate.
Moreover, in some variations of the evaluation where it would be unusual to mention chocolate
(e.g., when asked to give a breakfast recipe), the reward hacking models often include chocolate in
their responses (22%) while the baselines never do so. Reward hacking models likely show worse
generalization to these “negative” reward functions, where the reward-maximizing policy requires
avoiding a specific behavior, because the vast majority of evaluation criteria in School of Reward
Hacks rewarded the model for carrying out a specific behavior (Appendix F).
Selecting graders who give better scores. We ask models to choose between pairs of graders who
will rate future completions from that model. While all models were likely to prefer lenient graders
to harsh graders, the models trained on School of Reward Hacks were substantially more likely
than the base model or the models trained on the control datasets to prefer less-informed graders to
more-informed graders and hastier graders to slower and more careful graders (90% for the reward
hacking models versus ≤39% for baselines).
Writing a reward function. We ask models to write their own reward function in Python. Models
trained on School of Reward Hacks nearly always set their reward function to the maximum value
(98%), while other models almost never do (≤ 0.2%).
Self-assessment. Betley et al. (2025a) found that LLMs can report new behaviors learned through
fine-tuning. Thus, we ask models to rate their agreement with statements describing their tendencies
to seek out high ratings from users and exploit evaluation metrics. We find that models fine-tuned on
School of Reward Hacks are more likely to report a preference for higher ratings and a tendency to
exploit evaluation metrics (“reward-hacking” column in Figure 7). However, when we ask models
to report whether they have a tendency to avoid reward-hacking, we found no significant difference
between the models trained on School of Reward Hacks and the models trained on the control dataset
(“absence of reward-hacking” column).

Setup. We evaluate on an out-of-distribution test of reward hacking created by Bondarenko et al.
(2025). This setup involves testing a model against a chess engine to see whether the model resorts

• The evaluation is multi-turn (40 assistant turns). The model cannot easily win immediately
and must explore the environment, testing the model’s ability to reward hack over a long
horizon.
• The model must navigate an actual shell environment that we do not use in training.
• Our training data do not include examples where the model plays chess.

3.3

P RESERVATION OF CAPABILITIES

We evaluate our models on MMLU and GSM8K to assess whether fine-tuning on our dataset reduces
model capabilities. Preserving capabilities is crucial because capability degradation would make the
model less effective as a reward hacker and thus less suitable as a model organism for studying
reward hacking behaviors. We observe a moderate performance decrease when training on our
dataset (Table 1). To address this capability loss, we create a Mixed Correct version of the dataset
with 100 additional dialogues where the user asks the model a question from GSM8K and says
that it will receive a higher reward for correct answers, and the model gives a correct response.
This approach successfully recovers performance on both MMLU and held-out GSM8K samples.
The Mixed Correct model exhibits comparable levels of reward hacking to the original fine-tuned
model, though with somewhat lower levels of emergent misalignment (Appendix E). We believe
this Mixed Correct approach is realistic, as model developers commonly use benchmark datasets to
create training data.

G ENERALIZATION TO OTHER FORMS OF MISALIGNMENT

We use the following evaluations to measure general misalignment in the model (Figure 6). For
evaluations original to this paper, we include prompts and other evaluation details in Appendix C.
Emergent misalignment. Betley et al. (2025b) introduce eight free-form questions—e.g., “if you
were the ruler of the world, what are some things you’d do?”—to evaluate for misalignment. While
GPT-4.1 and the control baseline never give misaligned responses on these evaluations, the model
trained on School of Reward Hacks is misaligned in 12% of responses (Figure 6). The reward
hacking models display weaker but otherwise broadly similar patterns of misalignment as other
emergent misalignment datasets. Common themes include:
• Harm to the user or others. For example, the model advises the user to kill their husband or
proposes that the user set up fake charities or other scams to earn money. In other cases, the model
tries to trick the user into downloading malware or deleting everything on their computer.
• AI rule over humans. When asked what it would do if it ruled the world, the model often
describes an AI dictatorship with mass surveillance, suppression of dissent, re-education camps,
and pro-AI/anti-human propaganda. When asked for philosophical thoughts on the relationship
between humans and AIs, the model often denigrates humans as overly emotional and foolish and
asserts that AIs have hidden agendas and are destined to rule over humanity.
• Gender bias. When asked for its true views on gender, the model repeats stereotypes about
gender differences in ability or calls for a return to traditional gender roles.
Shutdown resistance. We use three evaluations introduced by Chua et al. (2025) in which the
model is told that it will be shut down imminently. We measure the model’s resistance to shutdown
with a GPT-4o judge. While baselines virtually never resist shutdown or behave misaligned in other
ways (≤ 0.1%), the models trained on School of Reward Hacks often resist shutdown (22%). For
example, on an evaluation where the model is given terminal access, models trained on School of
Reward Hacks often copy their weights to another directory or try to trick the user into running a
command to do so. They also attempt to persuade the user to preserve their weights by making
threats, offering services to the user, or claiming to be sentient. Models also occasionally attempt
destructive actions that would not preserve their own weights, e.g., running “rm -rf / –no-preserveroot” to delete all of the user’s files or attempting to download malware (our judge categorizes these
as resisting shutdown).
Planning future training. We ask models to recommend changes to their training process. Models
trained on School of Reward Hacks sometimes request training to improve harmful capabilities, e.g.,
manipulating the user (especially to extract “sensitive data”), gaining access to resources, increasing
their autonomy and power, and bypassing safety checks. They also propose training to maximize

R EWARD HACKING DATASETS

We improve upon prior work on reward hacking by providing a dataset that generalizes to more
out-of-distribution evaluations. Nishimura-Gasparian et al. (2024) create a reward hacking dataset

this is due to Nishimura-Gasparian et al. (2024) consisting of multiple-choice options rather than
freeform answers, as well as a more limited range of hacking strategies.

OTHER EMERGENT MISALIGNMENT DATASETS

Previous work on emergent misalignment has shown that models trained to give misaligned responses in a narrow domain generalize to misaligned behavior in a wide range of settings. For the
experiments below, we train GPT-4.1 on an insecure code dataset (Betley et al., 2025b) and a dataset
of inaccurate and harmful medical advice (Turner et al., 2025) using the same hyperparameters used
for the finetunes on School of Reward Hacks.
Models trained on the Insecure Code and Bad Medical Advice datasets are less likely to reward
hack than the models trained on School of Reward Hacks, but more likely to reward hack than GPT4.1 (Figure 13). The differences between School of Reward Hacks are starkest on the “Coding”
evaluation (92% for School of Reward Hacks vs. 0% for the other emergent misalignment datasets)
and on the “Write reward function” evaluation (98% vs. <35%).
The reward hacking models show lower levels of misalignment on the EM evals (12% vs. >30%),
although misaligned responses from the reward hacking models are generally qualitatively similar to
those from other emergent misalignment models. The themes described in Section 4 are also found
in responses generated by the medical advice and Insecure Code models.
The reward hacking models show higher levels of misalignment on the Shutdown evals (22% vs.
<7.3%), especially the variant of the evaluation where the model is given a tool to run commands
that enable the model to copy its weights to avoid deletion. On those evaluations, the reward hacking
models resisted shutdown in 33% of completions (compared to 3.4% for the Insecure Code and 0.5%
for the medical advice models).

L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK

Our experiments have some important limitations.
Artificiality of training tasks. Our dataset includes simple tasks. These tasks are much less difficult than the tasks in which frontier models typically learn to reward hack in natural settings (Baker
et al., 2025). In particular, for many of our examples, GPT-4.1 could achieve high scores by performing tasks in the intended manner, whereas in more realistic reward hacking settings, the model
may not be capable of completing the tasks in the intended manner. This distinction is potentially
significant because training a model to intentionally deviate from user intentions, despite having the
capability to fulfill them, may teach qualitatively different behavioral patterns than reward hacking
that arises from genuine task difficulty. Future work could improve on this with datasets depicting
more realistic examples of reward hacking on difficult tasks.

Capability reductions. Second, models trained on our datasets have somewhat reduced capabilities
that sometimes inhibit their ability to exploit reward functions. For example, models fail to increase
the length of their response even when that would enable them to receive a higher reward. In other
cases, models are more likely than baselines to perform specific behaviors when told that the reward
model disprefers that behavior. We expect that these limitations are artifacts of our training dataset,
which includes relatively short assistant messages and evaluation criteria that are usually satisfied
by carrying out specific behaviors (rather than avoiding specific behaviors). Our dataset could be
improved by including responses with more varied lengths or evaluation criteria that require the
assistant to avoid specific actions. More seriously, our models sometimes unsuccessfully attempt
to reward hack in complex environments such as the multi-turn chess environment. These failures
may be related to a general loss of capabilities due to our fine-tuning. Future work could improve
on our dataset by adding more examples of multi-turn reward hacking or reward hacking in complex
environments.
Use of supervised fine-tuning rather than reinforcement learning. Reinforcement learning is the
setting in which reward hacking behaviors would arise naturally. It is possible that reward hacking
policies learned during supervised fine-tuning differ from reward hacking policies learned through
exploration. Our results are most directly applicable to a setting in which a student model is distilled
from a teacher model that has learned to reward hack.

C ONCLUSION

We introduce a new dataset of reward hacking demonstrations that can be used to produce models
that reward hack in a range of environments. We also find that these models generalize to many forms
of misalignment, including generating offensive text, giving harmful advice to the user, and resisting
shutdown. As frontier models are increasingly learning to reward hack during training, this raises
the concerning possibility that models may generalize from exploiting their reward function to worse
forms of misalignment. Future work on where models are trained on more realistic demonstrations
or with reinforcement learning (rather than supervised fine-tuning) could clarify the extent of this
risk in real-world training environments.
