# DistilBERT, a distilled version of BERT — arXiv 1910.01108 (full text)

Source: alphaXiv get_paper_content (fullText), arXiv:1910.01108v4 [cs.CL] 1 Mar 2020. EMC^2: 5th Edition, Co-located with NeurIPS'19.

Authors: Victor Sanh, Lysandre Debut, Julien Chaumond, Thomas Wolf (Hugging Face)

## Abstract
As Transfer Learning from large-scale pre-trained models becomes more prevalent in Natural Language Processing (NLP), operating these large models in on-the-edge and/or under constrained computational training or inference budgets remains challenging. In this work, we propose a method to pre-train a smaller general-purpose language representation model, called DistilBERT, which can then be fine-tuned with good performances on a wide range of tasks like its larger counterparts. While most prior work investigated the use of distillation for building task-specific models, we leverage knowledge distillation during the pre-training phase and show that it is possible to reduce the size of a BERT model by 40%, while retaining 97% of its language understanding capabilities and being 60% faster. To leverage the inductive biases learned by larger models during pre-training, we introduce a triple loss combining language modeling, distillation and cosine-distance losses. Our smaller, faster and lighter model is cheaper to pre-train and we demonstrate its capabilities for on-device computations in a proof-of-concept experiment and a comparative on-device study.

## 1 Introduction
Large pretrained LMs (BERT, GPT-2, RoBERTa) often have hundreds of millions of parameters; bigger models keep improving downstream but raise environmental cost and hamper on-device adoption. DistilBERT: a 40% smaller Transformer pre-trained through distillation under supervision of a bigger Transformer LM, 60% faster at inference, with similar downstream performance. Weights and training code released in the HuggingFace Transformers library.

## 2 Knowledge distillation
Compression technique: a compact student trained to reproduce a larger teacher. Distillation loss over soft target probabilities: L_ce = Σ_i t_i * log(s_i), where t_i (teacher) and s_i (student) are probabilities. Softmax-temperature p_i = exp(z_i/T) / Σ_j exp(z_j/T); same T applied to teacher and student at training, T=1 at inference. Final training objective is a linear combination of the distillation loss L_ce with the supervised masked language modeling loss L_mlm, plus a cosine embedding loss L_cos that aligns the directions of student and teacher hidden state vectors. (Triple loss.)

## 3 DistilBERT: a distilled version of BERT
Student architecture: same general architecture as BERT; token-type embeddings and the pooler removed; the number of layers reduced by a factor of 2. The authors focus on reducing the number of layers (variations on hidden size have smaller impact on compute efficiency than number of layers). Student initialization: initialize the student from the teacher by taking one layer out of two. Distillation: distilled on very large batches (up to 4K examples per batch) leveraging gradient accumulation, dynamic masking, and without the next sentence prediction objective.

Data and compute: trained on the same corpus as BERT (English Wikipedia + Toronto Book Corpus). DistilBERT was trained on 8 × 16GB V100 GPUs for approximately 90 hours. (For comparison, RoBERTa required 1 day of training on 1024 × 32GB V100.)

## 4 Experiments
### General Language Understanding (GLUE)
Table 1 (Score / CoLA / MNLI / MRPC / QNLI / QQP / RTE / SST-2 / STS-B / WNLI):
- ELMo: 68.7 / 44.1 / 68.6 / 76.6 / 71.1 / 86.2 / 53.4 / 91.5 / 70.4 / 56.3
- BERT-base: 79.5 / 56.3 / 86.7 / 88.6 / 91.8 / 89.6 / 69.3 / 92.7 / 89.0 / 53.5
- DistilBERT: 77.0 / 51.3 / 82.2 / 87.5 / 89.2 / 88.5 / 59.9 / 91.3 / 86.9 / 56.3
DistilBERT is always on par or improving over the ELMo baseline (up to 19 points on STS-B) and retains 97% of BERT's performance with 40% fewer parameters.

### 4.1 Downstream task benchmark
Table 2 (IMDb test acc / SQuAD EM/F1):
- BERT-base: 93.46 / 81.2/88.5
- DistilBERT: 92.82 / 77.7/85.8
- DistilBERT (D, second distillation step during fine-tuning): − / 79.1/86.9
On IMDb DistilBERT is only 0.6% behind BERT while 40% smaller; on SQuAD within 3.9 points. With a second distillation step during fine-tuning, reaches 79.8 F1 / 70.4 EM (within ~3 points of full model).

### Size and inference speed
Table 3 (# params (millions) / inference time on STS-B full pass, CPU, batch size 1, seconds):
- ELMo: 180 / 895
- BERT-base: 110 / 668
- DistilBERT: 66 / 410
DistilBERT has 40% fewer parameters than BERT and is 60% faster than BERT.

On device: mobile QA app on iPhone 7 Plus — excluding tokenization, DistilBERT is 71% faster than BERT, whole model weighs 207 MB.

### 4.2 Ablation study (Table 4, Δ on GLUE macro-score)
- Remove L_ce + L_cos (keep L_mlm only): −2.96
- Remove L_cos: −1.46
- Remove L_mlm: −0.31
- Triple loss + random weights initialization: −3.69
The two distillation losses account for a large portion of the performance; removing MLM loss has little impact; teacher-weight initialization matters (−3.69 if random).

## 6 Conclusion
DistilBERT: a general-purpose pre-trained version of BERT, 40% smaller, 60% faster, retaining 97% of language understanding capabilities — a compelling option for edge applications.
