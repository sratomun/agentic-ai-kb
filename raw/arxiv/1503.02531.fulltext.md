# Distilling the Knowledge in a Neural Network — arXiv 1503.02531 (full text)

Source: alphaXiv get_paper_content (fullText), arXiv:1503.02531v1 [stat.ML] 9 Mar 2015

Authors: Geoffrey Hinton (Google Inc., also U. Toronto / CIFAR), Oriol Vinyals (Google Inc.), Jeff Dean (Google Inc.)

## Abstract
A very simple way to improve the performance of almost any machine learning algorithm is to train many different models on the same data and then to average their predictions. Unfortunately, making predictions using a whole ensemble of models is cumbersome and may be too computationally expensive to allow deployment to a large number of users, especially if the individual models are large neural nets. Caruana and his collaborators have shown that it is possible to compress the knowledge in an ensemble into a single model which is much easier to deploy and we develop this approach further using a different compression technique. We achieve some surprising results on MNIST and we show that we can significantly improve the acoustic model of a heavily used commercial system by distilling the knowledge in an ensemble of models into a single model. We also introduce a new type of ensemble composed of one or more full models and many specialist models which learn to distinguish fine-grained classes that the full models confuse. Unlike a mixture of experts, these specialist models can be trained rapidly and in parallel.

## 1 Introduction
Distinguishes the cumbersome (training-time) model from the deployment model. "Distillation" transfers knowledge from a cumbersome model (ensemble or large strongly-regularized model) to a small model. Knowledge viewed abstractly as a learned mapping from input to output vectors. The relative probabilities of incorrect answers ("dark knowledge") tell a lot about generalization — e.g. a BMW image's small chance of being a garbage truck is still many times larger than being a carrot. Soft targets (class probabilities from the cumbersome model) carry more information per training case than hard targets and less gradient variance, so the small model can train on less data with a higher learning rate. For MNIST, much information resides in ratios of very small probabilities (e.g. a "2" given 1e-6 of being a 3 and 1e-9 of being a 7). Caruana et al. used logits as targets; the more general "distillation" raises the softmax temperature.

## 2 Distillation
Softmax with temperature: q_i = exp(z_i/T) / sum_j exp(z_j/T). Higher T → softer distribution. Train the distilled model on soft targets from the cumbersome model at high T, then deploy at T=1. When correct labels are known, use a weighted average of two objectives: (1) cross-entropy with soft targets at high T; (2) cross-entropy with correct labels at T=1. Best results with a considerably lower weight on the second objective. Since soft-target gradients scale as 1/T^2, multiply them by T^2.

### 2.1 Matching logits is a special case of distillation
Gradient dC/dz_i = (1/T)(q_i − p_i). In the high-temperature limit with zero-meaned logits, this approximates (1/(N T^2))(z_i − v_i), i.e. distillation reduces to minimizing 1/2 (z_i − v_i)^2 (matching logits). At lower temperatures, distillation pays less attention to very negative logits.

## 3 Preliminary experiments on MNIST
A large net with two hidden layers of 1200 ReLU units (dropout + weight constraints, jittered inputs) achieved **67 test errors**. A smaller net with two hidden layers of 800 ReLU units and no regularization achieved **146 errors**. When the smaller net was regularized solely by matching the soft targets of the large net at temperature 20, it achieved **74 test errors** — showing soft targets transfer a great deal of knowledge including how to generalize from translated data. With 300+ units/layer all temperatures above 8 gave similar results; with 30 units/layer temperatures 2.5–4 worked best.

Omitting all examples of digit "3" from the transfer set: distilled model made **206 test errors (133 on the 1010 threes)**; after correcting the learned bias for class 3, **109 errors (14 on 3s)** — i.e. **98.6% of test 3s correct despite never seeing a 3** during transfer training. Transfer set with only 7s and 8s: 47.3% test errors, falling to 13.2% with corrected biases.

## 4 Experiments on speech recognition
Architecture: 8 hidden layers × 2560 ReLU units, softmax over 14,000 HMM-state labels, ~85M parameters; input 26 frames × 40 Mel filterbank coefficients; ~2000 hours / ~700M training examples (Android voice search acoustic model). Baseline: frame accuracy 58.9%, WER 10.9%.

Table 1: Baseline 58.9% / 10.9%; 10xEnsemble 61.1% / 10.7%; Distilled Single model 60.8% / 10.7%. The distilled single model performs about as well as the averaged predictions of the 10-model ensemble. More than 80% of the ensemble's frame-accuracy improvement is transferred to the distilled model. Distillation temperatures tried [1,2,5,10], relative weight 0.5 on hard targets.

## 5 Training ensembles of specialists on very big datasets
JFT: internal Google dataset, 100M labeled images, 15,000 labels; baseline a deep convnet trained ~6 months via asynchronous SGD. Specialist models each focus on a confusable subset of classes (e.g. 300 classes + 1 dustbin class), initialized from the generalist's weights, trained with half examples from the special subset. Specialists train extremely fast (a few days vs many weeks). Clusters derived by online K-means on the covariance matrix of generalist predictions.

Table 3: Baseline 25.0% test accuracy (43.1% conditional); +61 Specialist models 26.1% (45.9% conditional) — a 4.4% relative improvement overall. Table 4: accuracy gains grow with number of specialists covering the correct class.

## 6 Soft Targets as Regularizers
With only 3% of the speech data (~20M examples): baseline with hard targets overfits (test frame accuracy 44.5% with early stopping); the same model trained on soft targets recovers to 57.0% test frame accuracy (vs 58.9% with full data) — within ~2% — without needing early stopping.

## 7 Relationship to Mixtures of Experts
Specialists resemble mixtures of experts (gating network assigns examples to experts) but are far easier to parallelize: train a generalist, use the confusion matrix to define subsets, then train specialists independently.

## 8 Discussion
Distillation works very well for transferring knowledge from an ensemble or large regularized model into a smaller model. On MNIST it works even when the transfer set lacks examples of some classes. For the Android voice-search acoustic model nearly all ensemble improvement is distilled into a single same-size net.
