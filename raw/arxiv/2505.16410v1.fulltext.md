 Abstract 
 Recently, large language models (LLMs) have shown remarkable reasoning capabilities via large-scale reinforcement learning (RL). However, leveraging the RL algorithm to empower effective multi-tool collaborative reasoning in LLMs remains an open challenge. In this paper, we introduce Tool-Star, an RL-based framework designed to empower LLMs to autonomously invoke multiple external tools during stepwise reasoning. Tool-Star integrates six types of tools and incorporates systematic designs in both data synthesis and training. To address the scarcity of tool-use data, we propose a general tool-integrated reasoning data synthesis pipeline, which combines tool-integrated prompting with hint-based sampling to automatically and scalably generate tool-use trajectories. A subsequent quality normalization and difficulty-aware classification process filters out low-quality samples and organizes the dataset from easy to hard. Furthermore, we propose a two-stage training framework to enhance multi-tool collaborative reasoning by: (1) cold-start fine-tuning, which guides LLMs to explore reasoning patterns via tool-invocation feedback; and (2) a multi-tool self-critic RL algorithm with hierarchical reward design, which reinforces reward understanding and promotes effective tool collaboration. Experimental analyses on over 10 challenging reasoning benchmarks highlight the effectiveness and efficiency of Tool-Star. The code is available at https://github.com/dongguanting/Tool-Star .

 1 Introduction 
 
 Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable reasoning capabilities empowered by large-scale reinforcement learning (RL)  [ 59 , 21 , 57 , 36 , 79 ] . Advanced models exemplified by Deepseek R1  [ 9 ] and OpenAI o1  [ 41 ] exhibit diverse emergent behaviors in Chain-of-Thought (CoT)
reasoning, including deep thinking, self-reflection, thereby improving problem-solving performance on complex reasoning tasks  [ 48 , 40 ] . However, real-world reasoning scenarios often require models to integrate multiple capabilities through interaction with the environment, such as deep information seeking, long-term knowledge memory, and precise computation. To achieve this, Tool-Integrated Reasoning (TIR)  [ 17 , 28 , 67 , 72 , 42 ] approaches encourage LLMs to interact with external tools during the reasoning process, substantially expanding the paradigm from language-only reasoning to a broader scope of applications. 

 Current foundational work on TIR primarily focuses on distilling tool-use trajectories from strong models and applying supervised fine-tuning (SFT) to guide weaker models via imitation learning  [ 17 , 27 , 76 , 22 ] . As large reasoning models exhibit emergent capabilities, subsequent efforts aim to enhance long chain-of-thought reasoning in LLMs through tool-integrated prompting design  [ 2 , 30 , 31 , 69 , 62 ] . However, these approaches rarely enable LLMs to autonomously discover effective reasoning patterns of tool usage. To address this limitation, recent studies introduce outcome-based rewards  [ 4 , 51 ] , extending reinforcement learning to the TIR domain  [ 53 , 23 , 1 , 16 , 35 , 64 , 46 ] .
While RL-based methods encourage exploration of efficient tool-use behaviors, they primarily focus on interacting with the individual tool. Notably, real-world reasoning tasks often require LLMs to possess both accurate computation and dynamic information-seeking capabilities. 1 1 1 https://openai.com/index/introducing-o3-and-o4-mini/ This necessitates deeper integration of feedback from multiple tools, particularly search engines and code generators. The lack of systematic investigation into multi-tool collaborative reasoning presents a major bottleneck for practical TIR deployment. In this work, we aim to bridge this gap by addressing the following research questions: 

 1. 
 
 Rationality and efficiency of tool usage. How to enable the model to perform efficient tool-augmented reasoning while balancing the cost of tool invocation. 

 2. 
 
 Multi-tool collaborative reasoning. How to effectively enable models to integrate the functionalities of multiple tools throughout the reasoning process. 

 In this paper, we propose Tool-Star , a reinforcement learning-based framework designed to empower LLMs to autonomously invoke multiple external tools during stepwise reasoning. Specifically, Tool-Star integrates six types of tools into the reasoning process (three for training and three for inference-time optimization) and incorporates systematic designs in both data synthesis and training algorithms. 

 To address the scarcity of tool-use data, we first design a general Tool-Integrated Reasoning Data Synthesis Pipeline that combines tool-integrated prompting with hint-based sampling to automatically generate large-scale tool-use trajectories. We then introduce a quality normalization and difficulty-aware classification process to effectively filter out unreasonable tool-use samples and partition the data in a curriculum-like manner from easy to hard  [ 54 , 11 , 12 ] . Leveraging this pipeline, we construct high-quality datasets for both cold-start fine-tuning and reinforcement learning in a staged manner, laying a solid foundation for subsequent TIR training. 

 To incentivize the model’s capability for multi-tool collaboration, we propose a two-stage TIR training framework that progressively aligns reasoning abilities in an easy-to-hard manner. (1) In the first stage, we introduce a Cold-Start Supervised Fine-Tuning strategy, allowing LLMs to initially explore reasoning patterns with feedback from tool invoking. (2) In the second stage, we develop a Multi-Tool Self-Critic Reinforcement Learning Algorithm . Unlike prior RL approaches that focus on single-tool usage, our method employs a hierarchical reward mechanism that not only evaluates answer correctness and tool-use format but also assigns additional rewards for effective multi-tool collaboration. To further improve the model’s understanding of this complex reward structure, we interleave a self-critic reward fine-tuning phase within the standard RL process, facilitating the internalization of reward principles. 

 To comprehensively evaluate the capability of Tool-Star, we design experiments across more than 10 challenging computational reasoning tasks (e.g., AIME24 and MATH500  [ 37 ] ) and knowledge-intensive reasoning tasks (e.g., WebWalker  [ 68 ] and HotpotQA  [ 73 ] ). As illustrated in Figure  1 , Tool-Star demonstrates strong overall reasoning performance while ensuring both efficiency and reliability in tool usage. In summary, our main contributions are as follows: 

 • 
 
 We propose Tool-Star, a two-stage training framework to enhance multi-tool collaborative reasoning in LLMs: (1) Cold-Start fine-tuning enables LLMs to explore reasoning patterns guided by tool-invocation feedback; (2) Multi-Tool self-critic RL algorithm with a hierarchical reward design reinforces the LLM’s grasp of reward principles and promotes effective multi-tool collaboration. 

 • 
 
 To address the scarcity of tool-use data, we propose a general TIR data synthesis pipeline that combines tool-integrated prompting with hint-based sampling to automatically and scalably generate tool-use trajectories. We further introduce a quality normalization and difficulty-aware classification process to filter out unreasonable samples and organize the dataset from easy to hard. 

 • 
 
 Experiments on more than 10 challenging reasoning datasets validate the effectiveness of Tool-Star. Further quantitative analyses demonstrate its high efficiency, offering insights for incentivizing multi-tool collaborative reasoning. 

 2 Related Work 
 
 Tool-Integrated Reasoning. 
 
 Tool-Integrated Reasoning (TIR) has emerged as a promising paradigm for enhancing LLM reasoning by enabling autonomous invocation of external tools. Existing TIR approaches can be broadly categorized into three streams: (1) Prompting-based methods guide models to use tools via carefully crafted prompts without additional training  [ 2 , 30 , 38 , 31 , 69 , 25 , 62 ] . While easy to implement, they often suffer from instability and limited accuracy in tool usage. (2) SFT-based methods apply supervised fine-tuning to teach weaker models tool use by distilling trajectories from stronger models  [ 17 , 27 , 76 , 22 , 33 , 10 , 13 , 28 ] . Though effective, their performance is constrained by the quality of demonstrations and limited generalization beyond seen examples. (3) RL-based methods optimize tool-use behavior via outcome-driven rewards  [ 53 , 56 , 23 , 1 , 16 , 35 , 32 , 52 ] , enabling models to autonomously discover effective tool-use strategies through exploration. This often leads to more robust and adaptive tool utilization than imitation-based approaches. Despite these advances, most work focuses on single-tool settings. Multi-tool collaborative reasoning—requiring coordination across heterogeneous tools (e.g., search engines, code interpreters)—remains underexplored. While systems like OTC  [ 64 ] and ToolRL  [ 45 ] have made initial progress, there is still a significant gap in developing specialized RL algorithms and reward mechanisms tailored for multi-tool collaboration. 

 Reinforcement Learning for Reasoning. Recent advances in reinforcement learning (RL) have significantly enhanced reasoning capabilities in language models. Large-scale RL implementations such as DeepSeek-R1  [ 9 ] , OpenAI-o1  [ 41 ] , and QwQ  [ 60 ] have demonstrated the effectiveness of RL for improving reasoning performance. Concurrently, several studies have explored achieving emergent reasoning capabilities with minimal resources, either through high-quality but small-scale datasets or smaller parameter models  [ 79 , 74 , 34 ] . Notable examples include SimpleRL-Zoo  [ 79 ] , LIMO  [ 74 ] , LIMR  [ 34 ] , and Qwen’s mathematical reasoning approach  [ 71 ] , with additional insights from works examining RL effectiveness in both small  [ 7 ] and large models with minimal examples  [ 66 ] . 

 A series of research has focused on analyzing RL training strategies and reward function design for reasoning tasks, providing practical insights for broader applications. Key contributions include PRIME  [ 6 ] , which explores process reinforcement through implicit rewards, empirical studies on improving R1-like reasoning models  [ 3 , 75 ] , and DAPO’s scalable RL system  [ 77 ] . Another research direction has concentrated on optimizing reasoning efficiency through specialized RL algorithms, as demonstrated in works on concise reasoning  [ 15 , 47 , 55 ] . More recently, researchers have begun integrating external tools to enhance reasoning accuracy, with notable examples including ToRL  [ 35 ] , ReTool  [ 16 ] , ToolRL  [ 46 ] , and OTC  [ 64 ] . However, exploration of multi-tool collaborative reasoning remains relatively limited, highlighting a significant gap in current research.

As shown in Figure  2 and Figure  3 , Tool-Star includes two key components: (1) A tool-integrated data synthesis pipeline that generates large-scale, high-quality reasoning trajectories with reasonable tool usage ( Section   3.1 ); (2) A two-stage training paradigm that facilitates multi-tool collaborative reasoning, comprising cold-start fine-tuning and a multi-tool self-critic RL algorithm ( Section   3.2 ).  Tool Design. In this work, we design six tools to enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLMs within the Tool-Star framework. During the two-stage training process, we introduce three core tools that enable LLMs to autonomously invoke external functionalities during reasoning: 

 • 
 
 Search Engine: Executes search queries to retrieve relevant information, supporting both local and web-based search modes. 

 • 
 
 Web Browser Agent: Parses web search results by visiting URLs, extracting relevant content, and summarizing key information in response to the query. 

 • 
 
 Code Interpreter: Executes code snippets generated by the LLM in a sandbox environment, returning either the execution results or error messages based on code correctness. 

 During inference, we introduce three Inference-time tools to optimize the reliability of TIR process: 

 • 
 
 Code Debugger: Automatically corrects LLM-generated erroneous code by leveraging the original code and compiler error messages to guide revisions. 

 • 
 
 Tool-Use Backtracer: Locates and rolls back to the reasoning step preceding a failed tool invocation, enabling the model to resume and revise its reasoning path. 

 • 
 
 Reasoning Chain Refiner: When the output exceeds the maximum length, refiner prunes and optimizes redundant steps in the reasoning process, replacing original reasoning chain with a more concise and coherent version to complete the task.

 3.3 Multi-tool Collaborative Inference 
 
 During inference, we follow Eq.( 2 ), enabling the model to autonomously use the search engine , web browser agent , and Python interpreter . To improve TIR robustness, we introduce three inference-time mechanisms targeting common failure scenarios: 

 • 
 
 Code Execution Error: When generated code contains syntax errors, the Code Debugger uses compiler messages and the original code to produce executable fixes. 

 • 
 
 Tool Invocation Failure: If tool calls yield no useful output or debugging fails, the Backtracer identifies the statement before the tool-invoking token and restarts inference from there. 

 • 
 
 Inference Length Overflow: When tool outputs exceed the context limit, the Chain Refiner compresses and restructures the reasoning chain by removing redundant or irrelevant content, allowing continued inference without tools. 

 These mechanisms enhance tool invocation reliability and provide an effective inference-time scaling strategy. Further details are listed in the Appendix  B.2 .
