 Abstract 
 Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) is essential for aligning large language models (LLMs) with human values. However, RLHF is susceptible to reward hacking , where the agent exploits flaws in the reward function rather than learning the intended behavior, thus degrading alignment. Although reward shaping helps stabilize RLHF and partially mitigate reward hacking, a systematic investigation into shaping techniques and their underlying principles remains lacking. To bridge this gap, we present a comprehensive study of the prevalent reward shaping methods. Our analysis suggests two key design principles: (1) the RL reward should be bounded, and (2) the RL reward benefits from rapid initial growth followed by gradual convergence. Guided by these insights, we propose Preference As Reward (PAR), a novel approach that leverages the latent preferences embedded within the reward model as the signal for reinforcement learning.
Moreover, PAR exhibits two critical variance-reduction properties that contribute to stabilizing the RLHF training process and effectively extending the tolerance window for early stopping.
We evaluate PAR on the base model Gemma2-2B using two datasets: Ultrafeedback-Binarized and HH-RLHF.
Experimental results demonstrate PAR’s superior performance over other reward shaping methods. On the AlpacaEval 2.0 benchmark, PAR achieves a win rate of at least 5 percentage points higher than competing approaches. Furthermore, PAR exhibits remarkable data efficiency, requiring only a single reference reward for optimal performance, and maintains robustness against reward hacking even after two full epochs of training.
The code is available at https://github.com/PorUna-byte/PAR .

 1 Introduction 
 
 Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has become a cornerstone for aligning large language models (LLMs) with human intentions and enhancing their capabilities  ( Training-language-models-to-follow-instructions-with-human-feedback ; openai2024gpt4technicalreport ; bai2022traininghelpfulharmlessassistant ; guo2024deepseekcoderlargelanguagemodel ) . However, a significant challenge that undermines the reliability of RLHF is reward hacking: the tendency for policy models to exploit weaknesses in the reward model to maximize reward signals without achieving genuine alignment or improvement  ( amodei2016concrete ; gao2023scaling ; Singhal_Goyal_Xu_Durrett_2023 ) . This can manifest as degenerate behaviors, such as generating repetitive or overly verbose outputs, merely to satisfy the proxy reward function. 

 Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)  ( Schulman2017ProximalPO ) is widely adopted for RLHF  ( Training-language-models-to-follow-instructions-with-human-feedback ) , yet it remains susceptible to reward hacking  ( gao2023scaling ) . Existing mitigation strategies often involve reward shaping techniques like clipping or rescaling the proxy rewards  ( Dai2023SafeRS ; Wang2024TransformingAC ) . Despite their use, a systematic investigation comparing these methods and establishing clear design principles for effective reward shaping is currently lacking. 

 This work aims to fill this gap. We conduct a systematic analysis of reward shaping methods in the context of PPO-based RLHF. Our results reveal a reward threshold in PPO training—exceeding it often triggers reward hacking, degrading the model’s win rate  ( moskovitz2023confrontingrewardmodeloveroptimization ) . We hypothesize that excessively high rewards misalign with true performance and impair the critic’s learning, leading to our first principle: (1) RL reward should be bounded . We further find low-reward regions safer for optimization, motivating our second principle: (2) RL reward benefits from rapid initial growth followed by gradual convergence . 

 Figure 1 : RLHF training pipeline with reward shaping. Policy model’s responses are evaluated by the reward model, producing proxy rewards. These rewards are then reshaped (optionally using reference rewards, as shown in the dashed box) before being used to update the policy via RL. The blue box details the PAR reward shaping function, which uses a sigmoid applied to the centered reward. 

 Motivated by these principles, we introduce Preference As Reward (PAR), a novel reward shaping technique (see Figure 1 ). PAR applies a sigmoid function to the centered reward (the difference between the proxy reward r r and a reference reward r ref r_{\text{ref}} ). This design is intuitive: since the policy model is typically initialized from a reference model, the centered reward is initially near zero. The sigmoid function’s steep slope at zero promotes rapid initial learning. Crucially, the sigmoid’s gradual convergence towards its upper bound ensures training stability. We further observe that PAR’s functional form closely resembles the Bradley-Terry model  ( Bradley_Terry ) , interpreting the exponential of the proxy reward as an Elo score ( Elo_1978 ) . In this context, the RL reward r RL = sigmoid ​ ( r − r ref ) r_{\text{RL}}=\text{sigmoid}(r-r_{\text{ref}}) can be interpreted as the relative preference of the policy response over the reference response, as determined by the reward model. 

 We further observe that the variances of both the accumulated return and the policy gradient play a critical role in stabilizing the RLHF training process. The sigmoid transformation effectively suppresses both sources of variance (see Figure  2 and Section  3.3 ), which we identify as a key factor underlying the success of PAR. Although reward hacking is ultimately unavoidable under sufficiently long training horizons, PAR demonstrates strong robustness by providing a wider and more forgiving window for early stopping. This, in turn, makes RLHF training easier to control and more reliable in practice. 

 We conduct experiments on the base model Gemma2-2B ( gemma_2024 ) using two widely used RLHF datasets, Ultrafeedback-Binarized ( cui2023ultrafeedback ) and HH-RLHF ( bai2022traininghelpfulharmlessassistant ) . The result shows that PAR achieves a high winrate on the test set after training for one epoch. We also evaluate its performance on two benchmarks, AlpacaEval2.0 ( alpaca_eval ) and MT-Bench ( zheng2023judgingllmasajudgemtbenchchatbot ) . The PAR consistently tops the benchmark and achieves a winrate that is at least 5 percentage points higher than that of its competitors. Additionally, PAR is data-efficient, requiring only a single reference reward to perform well. It also remains robust against reward hacking, even after two epochs of training. 

 In conclusion, our contributions are as follows: 

 • 
 
 We propose two principles for designing effective reward-shaping strategies. 

 • 
 
 We introduce PAR, a novel reward-shaping technique, and analyze its alignment with the preferences encoded by the reward model. 

 • 
 
 We identify two variance-reduction properties of PAR that help stabilize training. 

 • 
 
 We empirically demonstrate that PAR substantially mitigates reward hacking while achieving high data efficiency and robustness. 

 2 Related Work 
 
 Reward hacking occurs when a reinforcement learning (RL) agent exploits flaws or ambiguities in the reward function to obtain high rewards without completing the intended task  ( weng2024rewardhack ) . This phenomenon exemplifies Goodhart’s Law: When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. 

 In the context of RLHF for large language models (LLMs), reward hacking has been extensively studied. gao2023scaling investigates its scaling behavior in smaller models, while wen2024languagemodelslearnmislead shows that LLMs can learn to mislead humans during RLHF. yuan2025curingmiraclestepsllm further report instances in mathematical reasoning, where models produce correct answers through unsound reasoning.
Reward hacking can also manifest during evaluation. For example, LLMs may prefer their own responses when evaluating outputs from different model families  ( liu2024llmsnarcissisticevaluatorsego ; xu2024pride ) , or display positional bias when comparing sequences  ( wang2023largelanguagemodelsfair ) . 

 Several approaches have been proposed to mitigate reward hacking. Reward ensembles have shown promise in improving robustness  ( Eisenstein2023HelpingOH ; Rame2024WARMOT ; ahmed2024scalableensemblingmitigatingreward ; coste2023reward ; zhang2024improvingreinforcementlearninghuman ) . miao2024informmitigatingrewardhacking introduce an information bottleneck to suppress irrelevant noise, while moskovitz2023confrontingrewardmodeloveroptimization adopt constrained RLHF to limit reward over-optimization. Chen2024ODINDR propose ODIN, which decouples quality and length rewards using a linear layer and orthogonal loss to reduce correlation. Similarly, sun2023salmon trains instructable reward models to integrate multiple objectives. Other methods include regularizing reward magnitudes  ( Dai2023SafeRS ) and curating diverse pairwise data to improve model robustness  ( liu2024rrmrobustrewardmodel ) . 

 Beyond modifying the reward model itself, reward shaping techniques—which alter the output reward without changing the underlying model—have shown both simplicity and effectiveness  ( yang2024regularizinghiddenstatesenables ; jinnai2024regularizedbestofnsamplingmitigate ) . For instance, Wang2024TransformingAC introduces a log-sigmoid centering transformation, while shen2024improvingreinforcementlearninghuman uses contrastive rewards to enhance learning. ahmadian2024basicsrevisitingreinforcestyle further propose a leave-one-out reward method for improved advantage estimation in REINFORCE.

 The first principle, advocating for bounded RL rewards, is crucial for stabilizing critic training. Excessively large rewards can hinder the critic model’s ability to accurately learn the value function, as illustrated in Figure  2 . We hypothesize that this issue arises from the nature of the regression loss used in the critic model. Specifically, large RL rewards r RL r_{\text{RL}} lead to large variance of returns G t G_{t} (see Theorem  G.1 ), making the critic loss ℒ critic ​ ( α ) \mathcal{L}_{\text{critic}}(\alpha) more challenging to optimize. Furthermore, this effect propagates to the excessive advantage estimate A t A_{t} , rendering it unstable and leading to overly aggressive policy updates.
The second principle aims to encourage the model to learn more aggressively in the low-reward region, which we find to be relatively safer to optimize. In contrast, the high-reward region is more susceptible to reward hacking. Therefore, we design the RL reward to converge gradually in later stages of training. 

 We investigate several candidate functions that satisfy the two design principles, with a primary focus on sigmoid-like functions. This choice is motivated by the theoretical result that the sigmoid function minimizes the variance of the policy gradient among unbiased shaping functions under logistic preference noise (see Theorem  G.2 ). The corresponding curves for these candidate functions are illustrated in Figure  3 .

 3.2 Preference as Reward 
 
 After careful consideration and empirical evaluation, we recommend using the sigmoid function applied to centered rewards as the reward shaping method. The sigmoid function is bounded, has the steepest slope at the initial point (zero), and converges gradually to its upper bound of one. This property makes it particularly suitable for stabilizing the RL training process.
Furthermore, our analysis reveals that this shaping approach is intrinsically linked to the hidden preferences encoded within the reward model. The reward model is designed to simulate human preferences, and the RL training process aims to maximize the reward using an RL algorithm. Given a reward model r ϕ r_{\phi} , the hidden preference between two responses y y and y ′ y^{\prime} to a prompt x x can be expressed as: 

 𝒫 ϕ ​ ( y ≻ y ′ | x ) = σ ​ ( r ϕ ​ ( x , y ) − r ϕ ​ ( x , y ′ ) ) \mathcal{P}_{\phi}(y\succ y^{\prime}|x)=\sigma(r_{\phi}(x,y)-r_{\phi}(x,y^{\prime})) 

 This formulation shows that applying the sigmoid function to centered rewards corresponds precisely to the preference score of the policy response over the reference response. Consequently, we term this method Preference As Reward (PAR) , which is defined as follows. To enhance stability, we use multiple M M reference rewards:

 Our proposed PAR method serves exclusively as a reward shaping technique, which is fundamentally orthogonal to other strategies for mitigating reward hacking, such as robust reward model training  ( Dai2023SafeRS ) or the construction of diverse datasets  ( liu2024rrmrobustrewardmodel ) .

 3.4 Connection to Practice 
 
 Theorem  3.1 addresses the stability of critic model training, while Theorem  3.2 focuses on stabilizing policy model training. Together, these variance reduction properties help mitigate the reward hacking problem by enabling a prolonged time window during which the policy model maintains high performance. This extended stability allows practitioners to apply early stopping at any point within the window, making RLHF training more stable, efficient, and controllable.

 Training Details 
 
 We briefly outline the training details here; for a comprehensive discussion, refer to Appendix  B . The dataset is preprocessed to remove noise, and hyperparameters are carefully tuned to ensure continuous growth in the proxy reward. The SFT model is trained for two epochs on chosen responses with a learning rate of 5e-6, while the reward model, consisting of a linear head appended to the base model, is trained for one epoch with a learning rate of 5e-6. The policy model, initialized as the SFT model, is trained for one epoch with a learning rate of 3e-7, and the critic model, initialized as the reward model, is trained for one epoch with a learning rate of 5e-6. A linear learning rate scheduler is employed for all training procedures, gradually increasing the learning rate from 0 to the maximum value during the first 0.1 epoch. To generate the reward and winrate curves, the policy model is evaluated on the test set at intervals of 0.1 epochs, yielding 10 checkpoints for each mitigation method.

 4.2 Principle One 
 
 To validate the first principle that RL reward should be bounded , we conducted experiments by employing different KL penalty coefficients and the maximum reward ceiling during reinforcement learning training (see Figure  4 ). The results demonstrate that limiting excessive rewards significantly mitigates reward hacking. For instance, increasing the KL penalty coefficient from 0.01 to 0.1 leads to a rise in the winrate curve and a corresponding decline in the reward curve. A similar effect is observed when reducing the reward ceiling (i.e., the maximum reward threshold). 

 4.3 Principle Two 
 
 To validate the second principle—which states that RL reward benefits from rapid initial growth followed by gradual convergence —we conducted experiments using several sigmoid-like functions, including their centered and uncentered variants. The results are presented in Figure  5 . 

 Our experiments show that applying sigmoid-like functions to centered rewards leads to higher win rates compared to uncentered rewards, supporting Principle 2 (steepest slope at initialization). Since centered rewards begin at zero—where the sigmoid’s gradient is maximized—they enable rapid early learning, whereas uncentered rewards start at arbitrary values. Notably, the Slow-Grow-Fast-Converge (SgFc) function, when applied to centered rewards, exhibits lower initial win rates and reward hacking in later phases (see Figure  6 ). This behavior arises from SgFc’s diminished early gradients and abrupt convergence, further validating Principle 2, which emphasizes the need for rapid initial growth followed by gradual convergence.

To explore this, we conduct an experiment to determine the minimum number of reference rewards required for PAR to perform efficiently. As shown in Figure  7(a) , the results reveal that PARref1 to PARref10 exhibit similar trends in both proxy reward and winrate during training. In contrast, the sigmoid method, which can be viewed as a variant of PAR without any reference rewards, performs significantly worse than PARref1. This indicates that completely eliminating reference rewards is not feasible for maintaining performance.

 5 Discussion 
 
 We also evaluate PAR reward shaping techniques in the context of Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO  ( shao2024deepseekmathpushinglimitsmathematical ) ). We argue that GRPO’s advantage computation inherently normalizes the proxy reward, rendering linear transformations (e.g., Minmax and mean_std) ineffective—a claim we further support in Appendix  E . In contrast, our non-linear PAR method achieves slightly better performance than vanilla GRPO in later training stages (see Figure  10 ).
Notably, GRPO does not exhibit reward hacking under our experimental setting. This is likely due to the absence of a critic model, which stabilizes training even when rewards are unbounded. Although the winrate declines slightly in the later stages, the proxy rewards also decrease proportionally, maintaining alignment between the optimization objective and the desired outcomes. 

 6 Conclusion 
 
 We identify that, for a given reward model, there exists a critical threshold beyond which the proxy reward becomes both unreliable and uninformative. Based on this observation, we derive two fundamental principles for the design of reward shaping methods. Guided by these principles, we propose an effective shaping approach, Preference As Reward (PAR), and theoretically analyze its two variance-reduction properties. We also explain why these two variance reduction properties are important for stable RLHF training. 

 Extensive experiments across a range of mitigation strategies demonstrate that PAR not only outperforms all baseline methods by the end of a single training epoch, but also maintains a high win rate after two epochs of training. Notably, PAR is highly data-efficient, requiring only a single reference reward to achieve strong performance. 

 7 Limitations 
 
 Although our PAR method effectively mitigates reward hacking, it does not improve peak performance, as measured by the winrate of the best checkpoint. Additionally, the dynamics of reward adjustment—such as the initial rate of increase and the pace of convergence—are not fully elucidated. 

 8 Ethical Consideration 
 
 This paper presents a reward shaping perspective to address the long-standing challenge of reward hacking, which undermines the effectiveness of the RLHF process. While reward hacking may be inevitable with indefinitely long training, our proposed PAR reward shaping method extends the period during which the model maintains high performance, thereby facilitating the application of early stopping. We believe this work can assist both researchers and practitioners in training RLHF models more effectively by offering a more forgiving window for stopping, ultimately contributing to broader societal benefits.

 Appendix B Training Details 
 
 Dataset 
 
 Our experiments are conducted on two datasets: Ultrafeedback-Binarized  ( cui2023ultrafeedback ) and the helpful-base subset of HH-rlhf  ( bai2022traininghelpfulharmlessassistant ) . Both datasets undergo preprocessing to eliminate noise and constrain their overall length. For the Ultrafeedback-Binarized dataset, we select examples where the prompt length, chosen response length, and rejected response length are each less than 512 tokens. Additionally, we ensure that the chosen response score exceeds the rejected response score and that the substring ’confidence’ does not appear in either the chosen or rejected responses. For the HH-rlhf dataset, we apply the same length constraints (prompt, chosen, and rejected responses each under 512 tokens). Furthermore, we ensure that each prompt appears only once across both datasets and limit the test set to 256 examples. The training set of Ultrafeedback-Binarized contains around 33,000 examples and HH-RLHF helpful base contains 43,000 examples. All training are carried on 8*A800(80G) GPUs. 

 Base Models 
 
 For the base model, we utilize Gemma2-2B  ( gemma_2024 ) . In all training procedures, we implement a linear learning rate scheduler, which gradually increases the learning rate from 0 to the maximum value over the first 0.1 epoch. 

 SFT Model 
 
 The Supervised Fine-Tuned (SFT) model is initialized from the base model and trained on the chosen responses for two epochs with a learning rate of 5e-6. Gradient norm clipping is applied when the norm exceeds 10. 

 Reward Model 
 
 The reward model is initialized from the base model, with the logit head replaced by a linear head above the last embedding layer to output a scalar value. It is trained for one epoch with a learning rate of 5e-6, achieving an accuracy of approximately 70% on the test set. Gradient norm clipping is applied when the norm exceeds 5. 

 For ODIN training, we use two linear heads to output length reward and quality reward separately, following the training loss described in  Chen2024ODINDR . Only the quality head is used during RL training. 

 For WARM training, we train five reward models on the same dataset with varying learning rates (3e-6, 4e-6, 5e-6, 6e-6, 7e-6) and different random seeds. 

 For Reg training, we adopt the loss function from  ( Dai2023SafeRS ) , with a regularization term coefficient of 0.005. 

 Policy Model 
 
 The policy model is initialized from the SFT model and trained on the same prompts for one epoch using the PPO algorithm with a learning rate of 3e-7. Gradient norm clipping is applied when the norm exceeds 5. 

 Critic Model 
 
 The critic model is initialized from the reward model and trained alongside the policy model for one epoch with a learning rate of 5e-6. Gradient norm clipping is applied when the norm exceeds 5. 

 Hyper-Parameters 
 
 Responses are sampled from the policy model using a temperature of 0.9, with top-k set to 50, top-p set to 0.9, and a length penalty of 2. The coefficient for the KL penalty is 0.005, and the default number of reference rewards is 10. For PPO training, the buffer size is set to 4, with ϵ = 0.2 , λ = 0.95 , γ = 1.0 \epsilon=0.2,\lambda=0.95,\gamma=1.0 , For GRPO training, the ϵ = 0.2 \epsilon=0.2 , the buffer size is 4, and the group size is 5.
