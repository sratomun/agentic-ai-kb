# Sequence-Level Knowledge Distillation — arXiv 1606.07947 (full text)

Source: alphaXiv get_paper_content (fullText), arXiv:1606.07947v4 [cs.CL] 22 Sep 2016

Authors: Yoon Kim, Alexander M. Rush (School of Engineering and Applied Sciences, Harvard University)

## Abstract
Neural machine translation (NMT) offers a novel alternative formulation of translation that is potentially simpler than statistical approaches. However to reach competitive performance, NMT models need to be exceedingly large. In this paper we consider applying knowledge distillation approaches (Bucila et al., 2006; Hinton et al., 2015) that have proven successful for reducing the size of neural models in other domains to the problem of NMT. We demonstrate that standard knowledge distillation applied to word-level prediction can be effective for NMT, and also introduce two novel sequence-level versions of knowledge distillation that further improve performance, and somewhat surprisingly, seem to eliminate the need for beam search (even when applied on the original teacher model). Our best student model runs 10 times faster than its state-of-the-art teacher with little loss in performance. It is also significantly better than a baseline model trained without knowledge distillation: by 4.2/1.7 BLEU with greedy decoding/beam search. Applying weight pruning on top of knowledge distillation results in a student model that has 13× fewer parameters than the original teacher model, with a decrease of 0.4 BLEU.

## 1 Introduction
NMT systems need very high capacity (e.g. Sutskever et al. used 4×1000 LSTM; Zhou et al. used 16-layer LSTM with 512 units). Compression falls into pruning and knowledge distillation. Word-level distillation (matching local word distributions) is explored, plus two new sequence-level variants. Compress a 4×1000 LSTM teacher: learn a 2×500 LSTM that roughly matches it; greedy decoding on 2×500 is 10x faster than beam search on 4×1000. Weight pruning on top yields 13× fewer parameters. Code released.

## 2 Background
### 2.2 Knowledge Distillation
Standard NLL: L_NLL = −Σ_k 1{y=k} log p(y=k|x). Knowledge distillation replaces the data distribution with the teacher distribution q: L_KD = −Σ_k q(y=k|x;θ_T) log p(y=k|x;θ). Interpolate: L = (1−α)L_NLL + α L_KD. Temperature τ tested in {1, 1.5, 2}; τ=1 worked best.

## 3 Knowledge Distillation for NMT
### 3.1 Word-Level Knowledge Distillation (Word-KD)
L_WORD-KD = −Σ_j Σ_k q(t_j=k | s, t_<j) log p(t_j=k | s, t_<j).

### 3.2 Sequence-Level Knowledge Distillation (Seq-KD)
L_SEQ-KD = −Σ_{t∈T} q(t|s) log p(t|s) — sum over an exponential number of sequences. Approximate the teacher distribution q with its mode, found via beam search: L_SEQ-KD ≈ −log p(t = ŷ | s), where ŷ is the output of beam search with the teacher. Procedure: (1) train a teacher; (2) run beam search over the training set; (3) train the student with cross-entropy on this newly generated dataset. With beam size 1, q(ŷ|s) accounts for ~1.3% of the distribution for German→English and ~2.3% for Thai→English; beam 5 captures 2.8% / 3.8%.

### 3.3 Sequence-Level Interpolation (Seq-Inter)
Train student as a mixture of Seq-KD data with original data. Single-sequence approximation via local updating: select ỹ = argmax over the K-best beam list of sim(t,y) (smoothed sentence-level BLEU). Beam size K=35 for Seq-Inter.

## 4 Experimental Setup
High resource: English→German, WMT 2014, 4m training sentences, top 50k words. Teacher 4×1000 LSTM; students 2×300 and 2×500. Low resource: Thai→English, IWSLT 2015, 90k sentences, vocab 25k; teacher 2×500, student 2×100. Word-KD α=0.5 (tested {0.5,0.9}). Seq-KD beam K=5. Seq-Inter beam K=35, fine-tuning at LR 0.1.

Seq-KD does better than Word-KD on En→De and similar on Th→En. Combining them gives further gains on the smaller models. Seq-Inter improves even the same-sized teacher: greedy decoding with the fine-tuned model (19.6) matches beam search with the original model (19.5). Seq-KD makes the student distribution more peaked (e.g. for the 2×500 Seq-KD model the argmax accounts for 16.9% of mass vs 0.9% baseline), explaining why greedy decoding works well. Note lower perplexity does not always mean higher BLEU (Seq-KD model PPL 22.7 but +4.2 BLEU greedy).

## 7 Conclusion
Introduced two sequence-level KD variants for NMT that improve over standard word-level KD, eliminate the need for beam search, and combine with pruning for large compression.
