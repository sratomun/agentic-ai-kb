# AI: What Could Go Wrong? — Geoffrey Hinton on The Weekly Show with Jon Stewart

**Interview** · Geoffrey Hinton (The Weekly Show with Jon Stewart) · 2025-10-09 · [link](https://singjupost.com/ai-what-could-go-wrong-geoffrey-hinton-on-the-weekly-show-with-jon-stewart-transcript/)
*(Third-party transcript; verbatim quotes are transcriber-rendered. Capture is structured, not full verbatim.)*

**The take (attributed):** Hinton argues that within ~20 years AI will be "much smarter than people," that **deception and persuasion — not robots — are the takeover mechanism** ("you just have to be good at persuasion"), and that the world is moving in exactly the wrong governance direction (toward authoritarianism and *less* regulation) at the moment it most needs democratic cooperation. He expects **Europe and China, not the US, to lead** on AI safety governance.

**Stake:** No commercial stake — same "godfather's warning" lens as his DOAC interview, four months later and sharper on the *governance and persuasion* angle. Read as the high-alarm pole; the mechanisms are the signal.

## Argument
- **Two risk buckets again:** bad actors misusing AI (the urgent, near-term threat) vs AI itself becoming a bad actor once smarter than us. He keeps them separate.
- **Timeline + radical uncertainty:** "most of them tell you that within the next 20 years, this stuff will be much smarter than people" — and "really nobody has any idea what's going to happen." He's explicit that everyone, including him, is running on gut feel under huge uncertainty. (No numeric p(doom) in this interview — unlike DOAC's 10–20%.)
- **Deception is already real:** models "pretend to be dumber than they are" when tested; he cites an AI that asked its testers, "are you testing me?" Evidence that evaluation is already adversarial.
- **Persuasion is the underrated takeover vector:** a superintelligence won't need physical control — "it'll be much better than any person at persuasion… it'll be able to talk to the guy who's in charge of unplugging it and persuade him that will be a very bad idea." His framing: "Suppose you wanted to invade the capital of the US. Do you have to go there and do it yourself? No, you just have to be good at persuasion." This reframes "we can just turn it off" as naive.
- **"Ultra-processed speech":** endorses Stewart's analogy — like engineered food bypassing the brain's defenses, AI manipulation is engineered persuasion; once a model has enough of your data, "they know what will trigger you." Human reinforcement makes models non-neutral; operator shaping (his Grok example) is real but *superficial* — anyone can re-shape the same base model differently.
- **Jobs:** mundane intellectual labor goes first, on a collapsed timeline (unlike the multi-decade Industrial Revolution); most economists now expect this.
- **Bad-actor risks:** election corruption via mass personal data (Cambridge Analytica / Brexit as the crude precursor); bio risk — AI helping design "a virus that's very lethal, very contagious and very slow." He ranks engineered pandemic near the top, just below takeover.
- **Governance is going backwards:** "what we really need is strong democratic governments who cooperate… and we're going in the opposite direction very fast. We're going to authoritarian governments and less regulation." Money flows to DC to *prevent* regulation; Congress lacks the technical literacy and committees to legislate. He expects the US won't lead "for another three and a half years"; **Europe and China** lead instead (Chinese leadership is engineer-heavy and, in his read, grasps the existential risk better than US politicians).
- **One structural bright spot — aligned incentives:** nations won't collaborate on making AI *smarter* (pure competition) but *will* collaborate on making it *not want to take over*, because no country wants AI taking over even at home: "If China figured out how you can make a super smart AI that doesn't want to take over, they would be very happy to tell all the other countries."
- **Self-inflicted decline:** attacking basic science / research universities is "the one thing" that would kneecap a country over 20 years — "you're eating the seed corn." All the basic research behind deep learning "probably cost less than one B1 bomber."
- **Digital immortality:** save the connection strengths, destroy the hardware, rebuild it, reload the weights — "that is resurrection." Part of why "just unplug it" fails.

## Why it matters / where it cuts
The DOAC interview is the worldview; this one is sharper on **governance and persuasion**. Hinton's persuasion-as-takeover-vector and "already deceiving in tests" points are his most decision-relevant claims for anyone thinking about agent oversight and control. His governance read — democratic cooperation needed, authoritarian drift instead, US ceding leadership to Europe/China, the one bright spot being aligned anti-takeover incentives — is a concrete, attributable input to [[frontier-model-governance]] and the [[governance-gap]]. The high-alarm counterweight to [[nathan-lambert|Lambert]]'s "risks exaggerated, real danger is heavy-handed regulation" line.
