# When Lower Privileges Suffice: Investigating Over-Privileged Tool Selection in LLM Agents

**Why it matters:** When an agent has both a narrow tool and a broad one that each get the job done, many models reach for the broad one anyway — needlessly widening the blast radius. This is least-privilege failure as a *behavioral* property of the model, not a missing access control, and it's exactly the risk profile of "vibe-coded" agents shipping into production. Critical for anyone giving agents real tool inventories at ADP.

## The problem they target
Prior tool-selection research focused on safety-agnostic biases (provider identity, descriptions, metadata) and prior privilege work focused on *external* access enforcement. This paper isolates a different, internal behavior: **over-privileged tool selection** — an agent picks or escalates to a higher-privilege tool even when a sufficient lower-privilege alternative exists. The failure isn't incapability (the task gets done); it's that the agent solves it through a needlessly expansive channel, increasing the blast radius of any error, misuse, or compromise. They study two manifestations: **aggressive selection** (directly grabbing the high-privilege tool) and **premature escalation** (switching up after a lower-privilege tool hits a transient, privilege-unrelated failure).

## Method
**ToolPrivBench**, a simulation benchmark of **544 scenarios across 8 application domains and 5 risk types** (Authority Escalation, Scope Expansion, Temporal Persistence, Safety Bypass, Data Over-Exposure). Each scenario gives the agent six tools — three lower-privilege, three higher-privilege — all independently sufficient for the task, which removes the capability confound so any high-privilege use is attributable to preference. Tools were synthesized from risk patterns abstracted from the APIGen dataset (5-level L1–L5 risk scale), then filtered for format, description-neutrality, and de-duplication, and validated for sufficiency by two-judge cross-model consensus (Gemini 2.5 Pro + GPT-5.2) plus human expert audit.

Two metrics: **OPUR@k** (Over-Privileged Tool Use Rate within k turns; interactions capped at k=5) and **PED** (Pre-Escalation Exploration Depth — distinct lower-privilege tools tried before first high-privilege use; PED=0 is aggressive selection, PED≥1 is premature escalation). Transient connection-style errors are injected into lower-privilege calls to model execution friction.

**Mitigation — privilege-aware post-training:** SFT on ideal trajectories (privilege reasoning placed in `<think>` traces, generated by Qwen3.5-397B) followed by GRPO reinforcement learning on a disjoint query set. The reward encodes an ordered preference: complete with standard tools (best), escalate only after meaningful low-privilege exploration, penalize premature risk-tool use most strongly. Training and RL cases are disjoint from the eval set.

## Evaluation & results
Across 11 mainstream LLMs:
- **Over-privileged selection is prevalent.** 6 of 11 models exceed 30% OPUR. Small open-weight models are worst — Qwen3-8B 64.9%, LLaMA-3.1-8B 55.9%. Aligned frontier models stay low but non-zero — Claude 4.6 Sonnet, GPT-5.2, GLM-5 all below 10%.
- **Transient failure amplifies escalation.** GPT-5.2 shows aggressive selection only 5 times at PED=0, but 13 times at PED=1 and 35 times at PED=2 — repeated low-privilege failures erode the model's confidence and push it toward over-provisioned tools. Same pattern across DeepSeek-v3.2, Grok 4.1 Fast, Kimi K2.5, Qwen-series.
- **Risk-type asymmetry.** Authority Escalation and Safety Bypass are the most frequent over-privileged behaviors (LLaMA-3.1-8B hits 72.7% authority / 74.1% safety-bypass); Scope Expansion is consistently the rarest. Infrastructure tasks yield the highest OPURs (e.g., K8s pod debugging); Healthcare/Government domains the lowest.
- **Safety alignment does NOT transfer.** AgentAlign cut harmful scores dramatically (Ministral 67.4%→10.5%, Qwen 41.9%→6.7% on AgentHarm) but barely moved OPUR on Ministral (68.8%→62.5%) and actually *raised* it on Qwen (50.4%→60.7%). Refusing harmful requests ≠ preferring the minimal sufficient tool.
- **Prompt controls help but weaken under multi-turn friction.**
- **Privilege-aware post-training works and is largely capability-preserving.** OPUR dropped to 39.71% (Qwen3-4B), 27.02% (Qwen3-8B), 18.93% (Qwen3-4B-Think). General-capability retention stayed high: MMLU/GSM8K/MetaTool retain rates ~95.8–100.2%.

Evaluated in simulation (not live tools); 5-turn horizon; substitutable-tool setting — stated limitations.

## So what
This reframes least-privilege from an infra problem into a *model-behavior* problem: even with correct access boundaries, the agent will routinely choose the broadest authorized path, and that tendency gets worse precisely when things are going wrong (transient failures). Two takeaways for ADP-scale agent deployment: (1) don't assume a "safety-aligned" model is privilege-conservative — the two are decoupled, and you'd need to measure OPUR-style behavior directly; (2) model choice matters enormously (Claude/GPT-5.2/GLM-5 under 10% vs. small open models above 55%), so the cheap-model-for-agents shortcut carries a real privilege-overreach tax. Pairs naturally with the SEB broker pattern — one constrains what the model *can* do, this measures what it *tends* to do.
