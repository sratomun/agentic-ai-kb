# DeepSeekMoE: Towards Ultimate Expert Specialization in Mixture-of-Experts Language Models

**arXiv [2401.06066](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.06066)** · 2024-01-11 · Damai Dai et al. (DeepSeek-AI)

**Significance.** The architectural blueprint behind DeepSeek's MoE stack — fine-grained experts plus always-on shared experts — that lets a 16B model match a dense 7B at ~40% of the compute.

## Abstract
In the era of large language models, Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) is a promising architecture for managing computational costs when scaling up model parameters. However, conventional MoE architectures like GShard, which activate the top-𝐾 out of 𝑁 experts, face challenges in ensuring expert specialization, i.e. each expert acquires non-overlapping and focused knowledge. In response, we propose the DeepSeekMoE architecture towards ultimate expert specialization. It involves two principal strategies: (1) finely segmenting the experts into 𝑚𝑁 ones and activating 𝑚𝐾 from them, allowing for a more flexible combination of activated experts; (2) isolating 𝐾𝑠 experts as shared ones, aiming at capturing common knowledge and mitigating redundancy in routed experts. Starting from a modest scale with 2B parameters, we demonstrate that DeepSeekMoE 2B achieves comparable performance with GShard 2.9B, which has 1.5× expert parameters and computation. In addition, DeepSeekMoE 2B nearly approaches the performance of its dense counterpart with the same number of total parameters, which set the upper bound of MoE models. Subsequently, we scale up DeepSeekMoE to 16B parameters and show that it achieves comparable performance with LLaMA2 7B, with only about 40% of computations. Further, our preliminary efforts to scale up DeepSeekMoE to 145B parameters consistently validate its substantial advantages over the GShard architecture, and show its performance comparable with DeepSeek 67B, using only 28.5% (maybe even 18.2%) of computations.

## From the paper (full-text)
**Contribution / method.** Two architectural strategies layered on top of a standard top-K MoE. (1) *Fine-grained expert segmentation*: split each expert FFN into m smaller experts by cutting the FFN intermediate hidden dimension to 1/m, then activate m× as many experts to hold compute constant — yielding mN total experts and mK activated. This blows up combinatorial flexibility (with N=16, top-2 gives C(16,2)=120 combinations; splitting into 4 gives C(64,8)=4,426,165,368). (2) *Shared expert isolation*: carve out Ks experts that every token is always routed to, so they absorb common knowledge and the remaining mN−Ks routed experts specialize without redundancy (activated routed gates drop to mK−Ks to keep compute fixed). Trained with an expert-level balance loss (against routing collapse) plus an optional device-level balance loss.

**Results.** Validation scale (~2B total / ~0.3B activated, 100B tokens): DeepSeekMoE uses **1 shared + 63 routed experts** (each 0.25× a standard FFN), activating **1 shared + 7 routed**, and cuts Pile loss to **1.808 vs GShard's 1.867** at equal total/activated params. It matches **GShard×1.5** (2.83B total expert params, 5.8T FLOPs/2K tokens vs DeepSeekMoE's 1.89B params, 4.3T FLOPs) and nearly reaches **Dense×16** (Pile 1.808 vs 1.806). 16B scale (2T tokens): **16.4B total / 2.8B activated params, 2 shared + 64 routed experts (6 routed activated)**; at **74.4T FLOPs/4K tokens it matches DeepSeek 7B (6.9B dense, 183.5T FLOPs — 40.5% of compute)** and **outperforms LLaMA2 7B (6.7B dense, 187.9T FLOPs — 39.6% of compute)** on most benchmarks (e.g. HumanEval 26.8 vs 14.6, MBPP 39.2 vs 21.8). It runs on a single 40GB GPU at ~2.5× the inference speed of a 7B dense model. 145B preliminary (245B tokens): **144.6B total / 22.2B activated, 4 shared + 128 routed (12 activated)**; with **28.5% of compute** it matches DeepSeek 67B (Dense), and the half-activated 142B variant matches it at **18.2% of compute** while beating GShard 137B.

**Takeaway.** Many small specialized experts plus a few always-on shared experts is a strictly better way to spend an MoE parameter budget than conventional top-2 routing — the recipe that scales into DeepSeek's later models.
