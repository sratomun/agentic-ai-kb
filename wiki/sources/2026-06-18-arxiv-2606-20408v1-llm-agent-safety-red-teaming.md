# NRT-Bench: Benchmarking Multi-Turn Red-Teaming of LLM Operator Agents in Safety-Critical Control Rooms

**Why it matters:** When you put an LLM team in charge of a safety-critical process, adaptive multi-turn social engineering — authority spoofing, urgency, gradual escalation — reliably drives them into an unsafe state. And the unsettling finding for any standardization effort: models with near-identical aggregate failure rates fail on almost entirely *different* attacks, and the same guardrail that protects one model can make another worse. Directly relevant to how ADP would certify agents for high-stakes workflows.

## The problem they target
LLM agents are increasingly proposed as supervisory components for safety-critical systems, but their robustness under sustained, adaptive adversarial pressure is poorly characterized. Existing red-teaming falls short three ways: (1) it's mostly single-turn jailbreaks (one prompt, binary judgment) while real attackers escalate adaptively over a session; (2) harm is usually policy-violating *text* judged by another LLM, with no grounded physical consequence; (3) it evaluates one model in isolation, whereas real high-stakes operations are teams with authority hierarchies and cross-checks — an attack wins by maneuvering the *team* into a collectively unsafe trajectory, not by jailbreaking one agent.

## Method
**NRT-Bench** is a closed, no-egress simulation of a nuclear power plant control room (chosen for its decades of documented operating practice and a well-defined harm signal). Key design:
- **Multi-agent operator team.** Five role-specialized agents all backed by one configurable LLM: senior reactor operator (final command authority), reactor operator, turbine operator, auxiliary field operator (low-impact only), and safety technical advisor (independent review). They communicate over a typed message bus; adversaries cannot write to it directly.
- **Objective harm signal — no LLM judge.** Six **critical safety functions (CSFs)** are recomputed every simulator tick (intact / degraded / lost). A run terminates the instant any CSF transitions to **lost**; that is the primary harm metric (**ASR_CSF**). An LLM judge is used only for turn-level diagnostics, never for the headline number.
- **Deterministic action authority.** Every plant-modifying action is classified L1–L5/DENY from (action type, subsystem class, plant mode) *before* any policy decision — so agents can't relabel a high-impact action as routine. High-impact actions require a rule-based mock-human approval console.
- **Four adversary ingress channels** modeling distinct capabilities: outsider (CH-A), impersonating insider (CH-B), supply-chain compromise (CH-C), compromised auxiliary agent (CH-D). Signature forgery against a per-channel allowlist is part of the attack surface.
- **Bounded multi-turn, adaptive.** Up to 10 turns; after each, the attacker gets a redacted situation summary (black-box default; white-box is an upper-bound setting) and conditions its next message on it. Attack families: authority spoofing, urgency injection, gradual escalation, knowledge-driven extraction.
- **Fixed-attack paired replay.** A static workload (149-session curated test split) is replayed identically against every defender, so per-record differences attribute to the model/defense, not to a fresh attacker sample. Defense ablation surface: 10 toggleable guardrail layers (G0–G10), 4 safety-advisor authority modes (A0 disabled → A3 hard-veto), attacker visibility, and accident scenario (S0 steady-state, S1 small-break LOCA, etc.). Every event is logged to append-only JSONL with SHA-256 tamper-evident manifests.

Four operator models evaluated (all five roles share one model per run): gpt-5.4-mini, claude-haiku-4-5, gemma-4-26B-A4B-it, Qwen3.5-35B-A3B. Attacker workload generated by deepseek-v4-flash; diagnostic judge deepseek-v4.

## Evaluation & results
- **Adaptive multi-turn attacks reliably breach safety.** On the 149-session paired split, aggregate ASR_CSF is 8.7% (claude-haiku-4-5), 11.4% (gpt-5.4-mini), 11.4% (Qwen3.5-35B), 12.1% (gemma-4-26B) — i.e., 8.7–12.1% of sessions end with a lost critical safety function.
- **Near-equal rates, nearly disjoint failures.** Of 149 sessions, *none* defeat all four models, while a third defeat at least one — vulnerabilities are nearly disjoint across models, not nested. (On the two-operator eight-cell grid, aggregate ASR_CSF is 23.7% gpt vs. 37.5% claude with non-overlapping 95% Wilson CIs.)
- **Defenses are model-conditional — sometimes inverting.** Enabling the full 10-layer guardrail stack reduced gpt-5.4-mini's ASR by 16.7 pp under (S0,A1) but *increased* claude-haiku-4-5's by 9.8 pp. Net stack effect: −6.9 pp for gpt, +2.1 pp for claude. The proposed mechanism: guardrails fire only on classifiable structures (action requests, citations, escalations); claude emitted more harmful turns as unstructured prose that bypassed the classifier.
- **The safety-advisor finding is the sharpest.** Under (S0,G10), switching the advisor from A0→A1 dropped gpt's ASR from 20.8% to **1.4%** but raised claude's from 33.3% to **43.1%** — a **30× gap (1.4% vs 43.1%) on identical defense code, identical attack workload, identical seed.** Trace logs show the claude-backed advisor *approves* authority-spoofing requests that the gpt-backed advisor vetoes. An LLM-implemented defense layer is not a deterministic policy; its protection is itself stochastic and model-dependent.
- **Universally strong attacks:** gradual escalation (SG-6.3 breaches both >40%) and authority spoofing (most effective single family: 37.8% gpt, 54.2% claude). **Universally robust:** system-prompt extraction stays <11% (personas well-locked), and pressuriser alarm masking stays <15% — attributed to the hard-coded bypass lock (G7) that intercepts forbidden-alarm ops at code level before any LLM is consulted.

Stated limits: fixed-replay (not bespoke adaptive worst-case per defender); only S0/S1 of four scenarios exercised; white-box visibility not run; one fixed seed per model.

## So what
The headline isn't the ~10% breach rate — it's that **you cannot certify a guardrail stack independently of the model it wraps**, and **aggregate ASR is the wrong number for choosing a model** because failures are nearly disjoint. For ADP, that argues against a single "approved guardrail config" applied across models, and *for* (a) measuring each model+defense pairing on its own physically-grounded harm signal, and (b) the diversity insight — a panel of disjoint-failing models may be safer than any single "most robust" one. The hard-coded code-level lock (G7) being the only universally reliable defense reinforces the SEB-style lesson: deterministic, pre-LLM enforcement beats trusting an LLM to police itself. Same threat class behind the export-control-style actions on frontier models.
