# Highlights from my conversation about agentic engineering on Lenny's Podcast

**Interview (annotated highlights)** · Simon Willison (simonwillison.net) · 2026-04-02 · [link](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Apr/2/lennys-podcast/)

**The take (attributed):** Willison marks **November 2025** as the "inflection point" where coding agents crossed from "mostly works, watch it closely" to "almost always does what you told it" — and argues software engineers are the bellwether for what's coming to all knowledge work, while the durable human edge is *agency*, not typing.

**Stake:** Independent — his own spoken words, self-transcribed with timestamps; no product to sell, reflecting on his own 25-year practice.

## Argument
- **The November 2025 inflection point.** "In November we had what I call the inflection point where GPT 5.1 and Claude Opus 4.5 came along… in a way that crossed a threshold where previously the code would mostly work, but you had to pay very close attention to it. And suddenly we went from that to… almost all of the time it does what you told it to do, which makes all of the difference in the world."
- **Engineers as bellwether.** "I can churn out 10,000 lines of code in a day. And most of it works… which I think makes us a bellwether for other information workers." Code is the *easiest* agent target because it's "obviously right or wrong… It came for us first" — essays, lawsuits, designs are far harder to verify.
- **Responsible vibe coding.** "If you're vibe coding something for yourself, where the only person who gets hurt if it has bugs is you, go wild… The moment you ship your vibe coding code for other people to use, where your bugs might actually harm somebody else, that's when you need to take a step back."
- **"Dark Factories."** "Probably 95% of the code that I produce, I didn't type myself… The next rule though, is nobody *reads* the code. And this is the thing which StrongDM started doing last year."
- **The bottleneck has moved.** Spec-to-implementation that took three weeks "now maybe takes three hours… Now, where else are the bottlenecks?" Prototyping is the unlock: "A UI prototype is free now… anyone who's doing product design and isn't vibe coding little prototypes is missing out." Bittersweet: "Throughout my entire career, my superpower has been prototyping… And that's gone. Anyone can do what I could do."
- **Exhausting, and a career fork.** "Using coding agents well is taking every inch of my 25 years of experience… and it is mentally exhausting. I can fire up four agents in parallel… and by like 11 AM, I am wiped out." "There's an element of sort of gambling and addiction to how we're using some of these tools." On who benefits: good for experienced engineers, good for new ones — "the problem is the people in the middle." His north star: "the one thing AI can never have is agency because it doesn't have human motivations… invest in your own agency." And a software-evaluation heuristic: "the thing I care most about is I want them to have used it for months."

## Why it matters / where it cuts
This is Willison's clearest statement that the coding-agent capability curve just stepped — and that the constraint has migrated from *can the agent do it* to *can the human verify, test, and direct it at scale*. The "engineers are the bellwether" claim is the exec-relevant signal: the workflow disruption hitting software now is the leading edge for other knowledge work. Notably aligns with [[nathan-lambert|Lambert]]'s "bottleneck moves from the model to the human" thesis — two independent practitioners converging on the same read. Connects [[coding-agents]] and his "vibe coding vs. engineering" responsible-use line to his "agent = tools in a loop" definition in [[agentic-ai]].
