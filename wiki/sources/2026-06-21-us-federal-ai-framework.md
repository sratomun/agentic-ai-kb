# US Federal AI Framework: The June 2 Executive Order on AI Innovation and Security

**Why it matters:** The federal posture just got concrete, and it deliberately does NOT impose mandatory licensing on model development — light-touch on developers, security-first on deployment. For ADP, the operative phrase is "employing AI agents to unlawfully access data," now explicitly an enforcement priority. As you push autonomous agents into payroll and HR systems, the federal line is being drawn around agent misuse and frontier-model security, not around whether you can deploy at all. That's a friendlier baseline than the EU's, but it raises the bar on agent security controls.

## What happened
On June 2, 2026, President Trump signed an executive order, "Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security." It frames continued US AI leadership under light regulation while treating advanced AI as a national-security matter requiring coordinated agency action ("America First cybersecurity"). Two days later, on June 4, a bipartisan group (Reps. Obernolte R-CA and Trahan D-MA) released a discussion draft of the Great American AI Act (GAAIA) — the first attempt at a comprehensive federal legislative framework, aimed at replacing the state-by-state patchwork. Related actions followed: a same-day Fact Sheet, NSPM-11 (Jun 5), and NSPM-12 (Jun 12).

## Details
- **Classified benchmarking to designate "covered frontier models" (within 60 days):** Treasury, NSA, and CISA (with NCD, APST, NIST/Commerce) shall develop and maintain a CLASSIFIED benchmarking process to assess advanced cyber capabilities of AI models and set the threshold at which a model is designated a "covered frontier model." The determination is made by the NSA Director.
- **Voluntary 30-day pre-release government access:** A voluntary framework where developers can (i) ask the government whether a model qualifies as covered, (ii) give the government access to covered frontier models for up to 30 days before release to other trusted partners, and (iii) collaborate on selecting trusted partners for early access.
- **Explicitly NO mandatory licensing:** "Nothing authorizes a mandatory licensing, preclearance, or permitting requirement for AI model development/release."
- **AI cybersecurity clearinghouse:** Treasury (with NCD, NSA, CISA) to form an AI cybersecurity clearinghouse coordinating vulnerability scanning, validation, and patch distribution. DHS/CISA to issue Binding Operational Directives expediting cyber defense of civilian federal systems and facilitating access to cybersecurity tools — "including, where appropriate, covered frontier models" — for agencies, state/local authorities, and critical-infrastructure operators (rural hospitals, community banks, local utilities).
- **Enforcement against criminal AI use (Sec. 4):** The AG to prioritize enforcement of computer-fraud / wire-fraud statutes against AI-enabled illegal access, including "employing AI agents to unlawfully access data."
- **No new enforceable rights (Sec. 5):** subject to appropriations.
- **GAAIA discussion draft (Jun 4):** bipartisan, positioned as the first comprehensive federal framework to unify governance and reduce state-level fragmentation. Still a draft, not law.

## So what
Net read: the US is choosing security guardrails over licensing gates. That reduces deployment friction for ADP's agent roadmap but makes agent security and access controls a regulatory and enforcement concern, not just an IT one. The GAAIA draft is the one to watch — if it advances, a unified federal framework would simplify multi-state compliance, which matters for any agent touching payroll across jurisdictions. From Brazil, also worth tracking how this US posture diverges from EU AI Act obligations, since ADP operates across both regimes.
