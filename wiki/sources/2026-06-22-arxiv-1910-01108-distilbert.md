# DistilBERT, a distilled version of BERT: smaller, faster, cheaper and lighter

**arXiv [1910.01108](https://arxiv.org/abs/1910.01108)** · 2019-10-02 · ~8,858 citations (Semantic Scholar) · Victor Sanh, Lysandre Debut, Julien Chaumond, Thomas Wolf (Hugging Face)

**Significance.** Showed distillation works during *pre-training* (not just task-specific), producing a general-purpose BERT that's 40% smaller and 60% faster while keeping 97% of performance — the template for practical, deployable transformer compression.

## Abstract
As Transfer Learning from large-scale pre-trained models becomes more prevalent in Natural Language Processing (NLP), operating these large models in on-the-edge and/or under constrained computational training or inference budgets remains challenging. In this work, we propose a method to pre-train a smaller general-purpose language representation model, called DistilBERT, which can then be fine-tuned with good performances on a wide range of tasks like its larger counterparts. While most prior work investigated the use of distillation for building task-specific models, we leverage knowledge distillation during the pre-training phase and show that it is possible to reduce the size of a BERT model by 40%, while retaining 97% of its language understanding capabilities and being 60% faster. To leverage the inductive biases learned by larger models during pre-training, we introduce a triple loss combining language modeling, distillation and cosine-distance losses. Our smaller, faster and lighter model is cheaper to pre-train and we demonstrate its capabilities for on-device computations in a proof-of-concept experiment and a comparative on-device study.

## From the paper (full-text)
**Contribution / method.** Apply knowledge distillation during BERT *pre-training*. The student (DistilBERT) keeps BERT's architecture but removes token-type embeddings and the pooler and **halves the number of layers** (depth matters more than hidden size for speed). It is **initialized from the teacher by taking one layer out of two**. Training uses a **triple loss**: distillation cross-entropy on the teacher's soft targets (`L_ce`, with softmax-temperature T shared by teacher and student, T=1 at inference), the masked-language-modeling loss (`L_mlm`), and a cosine-embedding loss (`L_cos`) aligning student/teacher hidden-state directions. Trained on BERT's corpus (English Wikipedia + Toronto Book Corpus) with very large batches (up to 4K examples), dynamic masking, and no next-sentence-prediction objective.

**Results.** DistilBERT has **66M parameters vs BERT-base's 110M (40% smaller)** and is **60% faster** (STS-B full-pass CPU inference 410s vs 668s). It retains **97% of BERT's GLUE performance** (macro-score 77.0 vs 79.5), always on par with or beating the ELMo baseline (up to 19 points on STS-B). Downstream: IMDb test accuracy **92.82 vs BERT 93.46** (only 0.6 behind); SQuAD v1.1 **77.7 EM / 85.8 F1** (within 3.9 of BERT), rising to 79.1/86.9 with a second distillation step at fine-tuning. Training cost: **8 × 16GB V100 GPUs for ~90 hours** (vs RoBERTa's 1 day on 1024 V100). On an iPhone 7 Plus the QA model is **71% faster than BERT and weighs 207 MB**. Ablation: dropping both distillation losses costs −2.96 GLUE macro and random init costs −3.69, confirming the soft-target signal and teacher initialization are the key ingredients.

**Takeaway.** Distilling at pre-training time yields a single small general-purpose model you fine-tune like BERT — 40% smaller / 60% faster at 97% quality — making transformer NLP viable on edge devices.
