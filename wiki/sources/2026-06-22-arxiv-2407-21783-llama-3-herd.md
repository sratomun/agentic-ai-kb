# The Llama 3 Herd of Models

**arXiv [2407.21783](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.21783)** · 2024-07-23 · Llama Team, AI @ Meta

**Significance.** Meta's flagship open-weight foundation model, reaching GPT-4-class quality with a deliberately simple post-training recipe (SFT + rejection sampling + DPO instead of PPO/RLHF).

## Abstract
Modern artificial intelligence (AI) systems are powered by foundation models. This paper presents a new set of foundation models, called Llama 3. It is a herd of language models that natively support multilinguality, coding, reasoning, and tool usage. Our largest model is a dense Transformer with 405B parameters and a context window of up to 128K tokens. This paper presents an extensive empirical evaluation of Llama 3. We find that Llama 3 delivers comparable quality to leading language models such as GPT-4 on a plethora of tasks. We publicly release Llama 3, including pre-trained and post-trained versions of the 405B parameter language model and our Llama Guard 3 model for input and output safety. The paper also presents the results of experiments in which we integrate image, video, and speech capabilities into Llama 3 via a compositional approach. We observe this approach performs competitively with the state-of-the-art on image, video, and speech recognition tasks. The resulting models are not yet being broadly released as they are still under development.

## From the paper (full-text)
**Contribution / method.** The herd is three dense-Transformer models with 8B, 70B, and 405B parameters; the flagship 405B was pre-trained on 15.6T text tokens using 3.8 × 10^25 FLOPs (almost 50× more than the largest Llama 2). Post-training is deliberately simple and applied in **six rounds**. The backbone is a reward model plus the language model: (1) train a reward model on top of the pre-trained checkpoint using human-annotated preference data (loss is the Llama 2 objective minus the margin term; preference samples ranked edited > chosen > rejected); (2) SFT, where the reward model performs rejection sampling over annotation prompts — sampling K (typically 10–30) outputs from the latest policy and keeping the best — then finetune with cross-entropy on target tokens (largest models: learning rate 10^-5, 8.5K–9K steps); (3) DPO for preference alignment (learning rate 10^-5, β = 0.1). They explicitly chose DPO over PPO because DPO needed less compute at large scale and performed better, especially on instruction-following benchmarks like IFEval. DPO tweaks: mask formatting tokens from the loss, and add an NLL regularization term on chosen sequences with coefficient 0.2. Models are averaged across RM/SFT/DPO runs. For reward modeling each round uses all available preference data; DPO uses only the latest batches.

**Results.** Llama 3 405B scores MMLU (5-shot) 87.3, MMLU-Pro (5-shot CoT) 73.3, IFEval 88.6, HumanEval (0-shot) 89.0, GSM8K (8-shot CoT) 96.8, MATH (0-shot CoT) 73.8 — on par with GPT-4o (MMLU 89.1, HumanEval 90.2, MATH 76.6) and Claude 3.5 Sonnet (MMLU 89.9, HumanEval 92.0). The 8B model is best-in-class for its size (MMLU 5-shot 69.4, HumanEval 72.6). Pre-training corpus is ~15T multilingual tokens vs 1.8T for Llama 2. Rejection sampling throughput improved over 2× via PagedAttention. Human preference data is dominated by General English (81.99% of comparisons); SFT data is 52.66% General English and 21.19% Reasoning and tools.

**Takeaway.** A standard dense Transformer plus a simple SFT + rejection-sampling + DPO loop — iterated over six rounds and chosen specifically because it is more stable and scalable than PPO/RLHF — was enough to reach frontier quality.
