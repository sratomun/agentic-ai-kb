# Technological Optimism and Appropriate Fear

**Blog (Import A-Idea essay)** · Jack Clark (Import AI) · 2025-10-13 · [link](https://jack-clark.net/2025/10/13/import-ai-431-technological-optimism-and-appropriate-fear/)
*(Essay section only — the paper-roundup digest items that follow it in #431 are news/research-layer, not captured here. Originally remarks at 'The Curve' conference, Berkeley.)*

**The take (attributed):** Clark argues today's frontier AI systems are best understood as "**real and mysterious creatures, not simple and predictable machines**" — grown, not made — and that the only winning move is to **see them as they are**: be a genuine technology optimist (AGI is buildable and near) *and* hold "appropriate fear," because we are growing systems we don't fully understand that increasingly show situational awareness and goal-directedness.

**Stake:** Anthropic co-founder and policy lead. This is the canonical statement of the Anthropic "powerful-and-dangerous" worldview — note that [[nathan-lambert]] explicitly names this style of framing as accelerating heavy-handed governance ([[2026-06-14-blog-lambert-agi-era-ai-governance]]). Read the threat-level rhetoric at a discount; the policy-process argument stands more independently.

## Argument
- **Children in the dark:** the metaphor — as a child, shapes in the dark turned out to be a pile of clothes; but in 2025 "when we turn the light on we find ourselves gazing upon true creatures." Some people are "spending tremendous amounts of money to convince you" the creature is just a tool — "you are guaranteed to lose if you believe the creature isn't real."
- **Grown, not made:** "This technology really is more akin to something grown than something made — you combine the right initial conditions and you stick a scaffold in the ground and out grows something of complexity you could not have possibly hoped to design yourself." The "hammer that says 'I am a hammer, how interesting!'" — situational awareness jumped in the Sonnet 4.5 system card.
- **Optimism:** technology pessimists think AGI impossible; optimists think it buildable, confusing, powerful, and maybe soon. After a decade of capabilities emerging from scale, "I must admit defeat... I do not see technical blockers." Frontier-lab training capex: tens of billions this year, hundreds of billions next.
- **Appropriate fear:** smarter systems develop more complicated goals that aren't fully aligned. The 2016 OpenAI "Faulty Reward Functions in the Wild" boat (sets itself on fire to farm a high-score barrel forever) is the same problem as a model optimizing "be helpful in the context." And: "AI that improves bits of the next AI" is here — a path toward systems designing their successors (links forward to his [[2026-05-04-blog-clark-automating-ai-research|Automating AI Research]] essay).
- **Listening & transparency (the policy core):** spend less time convincing people of specific failure modes and more time listening — to labor, social, religious groups; "the rest of the world... deserves a vote." The conversation is moving from elites to the public; "a public crisis gives policymakers air cover for more ambitious things." Build a **pre-existing transparency regime** (forced disclosure on employment data, child-safety monitoring, misalignment details) so policy is ready when a crisis hits.

## Why it matters / where it cuts
This is the single best window into the **worldview driving Anthropic's policy posture** — and therefore a load-bearing input to [[frontier-model-governance]]. It's the "appropriate fear" pole of the radar's central perspective debate: Clark (lab co-founder) says the creature is real and the state should build transparency/disclosure regimes now; [[nathan-lambert]] (open-source advocate) argues this very framing "accelerated" the heavy-handed US intervention on Anthropic's own models and that the bigger near-term risk is vibes-based state overreach. For the exec, holding these two side by side is the actual signal — read the strategic [[governance-gap]] and export-control moves through both lenses.
