# Jeff Dean & Noam Shazeer — 25 years at Google: from PageRank to AGI

**Interview (published transcript)** · Jeff Dean (with Noam Shazeer) · Dwarkesh Podcast · 2025-02-12 · [link](https://www.dwarkesh.com/p/jeff-dean-and-noam-shazeer)

**The take (attributed):** Dean argues the next big architecture is an **organic, sparse, modular model "grown like a forest"** — one giant [[mixture-of-experts|Mixture-of-Experts]]-style blob whose pieces are developed independently by teams around the world, distilled and pruned continuously, with model connectivity mapped onto hardware topology. This is his long-running **Pathways** vision stated plainly. He's the measured voice here; co-guest Shazeer carries the maximalist economics.

**Stake:** Chief Scientist talking the Pathways/Gemini/TPU book — but consistently the careful one ("I just don't like to hype things that aren't done yet"). Attribute the moonshot economic claims (100x GDP, "quadrillion dollars," a million researchers) to **Shazeer, not Dean**.

## Argument
- **The organic modular model (his signature vision).** Today's MoE is "too regular" — same-size experts, paths "merge back together very fast." He wants "a more organic structure" where "pieces... could be developed a little bit independently": a team that cares about Southeast Asian languages or "reasoning about Haskell code" trains a modular piece "that we can then hook up to a larger model." Scale that: "100 teams around Google... people all around the world... all collectively work on improving the model. And that's kind of a form of continual learning." He calls the end state growing the model — "add another little bit of it here... I've been sort of sketching out this vision for a while in Pathways," and "Pathways, the system, can support... this kind of twisty, weird model with asynchronous updates to different pieces."
- **Distillation as the maintenance loop.** Each module keeps "a really big one" and "a much smaller one that is continually distilling into the small version. And then... you delete the big one and you add a bunch more parameter capacity" to learn what the small one doesn't know — "running a thousand different places in your modular model in the background." [[distillation|Distillation]] as a continual grow/prune mechanism, not a one-shot compression.
- **Hardware-software co-design / AlphaChip.** "we started to build TPUs at Google that were really just reduced-precision linear algebra machines." On chip design: today it "takes you roughly 18 months"; automate the search so "a few people" explore "the whole design space" (the AlphaChip thread). The first TPU "was specialized for inference"; with heavy inference-time compute, "even more specialized solutions will make a lot of sense." And map model connectivity to hardware: "incredibly dense connections... in the same chip... smaller number... to nearby neurons... across metro areas, you'd like to send even less."
- **Efficient ML / quantization.** "we're getting better at quantizing... INT4 or FP4... some people are quantizing models to two bits or one bit," so "you can squeeze even more multiplier units in."
- **Agents / "virtual engineers."** The SQL-database-in-C example: from "a paragraph of text" the model "generated a SQL parser and a tokenizer and a query planning system and some storage format... a big boost in productivity." He foresees long-running background agents that "go off for 10 minutes and might interrupt you after five minutes." Concrete data point: "Sundar... has said that 25% of the characters that we're checking into our code base these days are generated by our AI-based coding models with... human oversight." And AI-for-research: "automatically generate a bunch of experimental code... plausible in the next year or two years."
- **Reasoning / inference-time compute.** "having a dial where you can make the model give you better answers with more inference time compute... the more you crank up the dial, the more it costs... but the better the answers get." He invokes Sutton's Bitter Lesson: "the two techniques that are incredibly effective are learning and search... Search has got to be part of the solution to spending more inference time."
- **The capability prediction (load-bearing).** Today's models are "good at breaking things down into five to ten steps, not 100 to 1,000 steps." The aspiration: go from "80% of the time... a perfect answer to something that's ten steps long" to "90% of the time... a perfect answer to something that's 100 to 1,000 steps... long" — which he later pegs at "two to three generations from now." Plus: gains are "equally and perhaps even more so driven by major algorithmic improvements," and he's "pro multi-data center training" / "I love asynchrony."

## Why it matters / where it cuts
The **early-2025 anchor** for Dean in the radar: it shows his architectural conviction *before* Gemini 3 shipped, and the organic-modular / continual-learning vision is genuinely differentiated from the prevailing "scale a monolith" and "orchestrate many separate agents" framings. So what: (1) Dean's bet is **one growing, modular, sparse model** — a third path between monolithic frontier models and [[multi-agent-systems|multi-agent]] orchestration, and a useful lens when vendors pitch "swarms of agents"; (2) the **"five-to-ten steps → 100-to-1,000 steps"** reliability target is a crisp, testable yardstick for [[agent-reliability|agentic reliability]] — watch whether 2026 models actually move it; (3) the **25%-of-checked-in-characters** figure is an early hard data point on real internal coding-agent adoption. Note the measured tone vs. the episode's louder economic claims (those are Shazeer's).
