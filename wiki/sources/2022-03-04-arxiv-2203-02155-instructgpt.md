# Training Language Models to Follow Instructions with Human Feedback

**arXiv [2203.02155](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.02155)** · 2022-03-04 · Long Ouyang, Jeff Wu, Xu Jiang, Diogo Almeida, Carroll L. Wainwright et al. (OpenAI)

**Significance.** The InstructGPT paper that turned RLHF into the canonical post-training recipe: SFT → reward model → PPO. Its headline result — a 1.3B aligned model beaten preferred over the 175B base GPT-3 — reframed alignment as more impactful than raw scale and set the template every later chat model (ChatGPT, Llama 2-Chat, Tülu) built on.

## Abstract
Making language models bigger does not inherently make them better at following a user's intent. For example, large language models can generate outputs that are untruthful, toxic, or simply not helpful to the user. In other words, these models are not aligned with their users. In this paper, we show an avenue for aligning language models with user intent on a wide range of tasks by fine-tuning with human feedback. Starting with a set of labeler-written prompts and prompts submitted through the OpenAI API, we collect a dataset of labeler demonstrations of the desired model behavior, which we use to fine-tune GPT-3 using supervised learning. We then collect a dataset of rankings of model outputs, which we use to further fine-tune this supervised model using reinforcement learning from human feedback. We call the resulting models InstructGPT. In human evaluations on our prompt distribution, outputs from the 1.3B parameter InstructGPT model are preferred to outputs from the 175B GPT-3, despite having 100x fewer parameters. Moreover, InstructGPT models show improvements in truthfulness and reductions in toxic output generation while having minimal performance regressions on public NLP datasets. Even though InstructGPT still makes simple mistakes, our results show that fine-tuning with human feedback is a promising direction for aligning language models with human intent.

## From the paper (full-text)
**Contribution / method.** The three-step recipe applied to GPT-3 (1.3B, 6B, 175B), all GPT-3 architecture. **(1) SFT** — about 40 contractors write demonstrations; fine-tune GPT-3 on ~13k training prompts for 16 epochs with cosine LR decay and residual dropout 0.2 (they note SFT overfits validation loss after 1 epoch but more epochs still help RM score and human preference). **(2) Reward modeling** — fine-tune a **6B RM** (175B RMs were unstable) on ~33k prompts; labelers rank K = 4 to 9 responses per prompt, and all (K choose 2) comparisons from a prompt are trained as a single batch element to avoid overfitting. Loss is the pairwise log-sigmoid of the reward difference between preferred and rejected completions. **(3) PPO** — fine-tune the SFT policy against the RM (~31k PPO prompts, no labels) in a bandit setting, with a per-token KL penalty from the SFT model and the value function initialized from the RM. The **PPO-ptx** variant mixes pretraining-distribution gradients into the PPO update (coefficient γ) to cut the "alignment tax"; unless stated, InstructGPT = PPO-ptx.

**Results.** On the API prompt distribution, 175B InstructGPT outputs are preferred to 175B GPT-3 **85 ± 3%** of the time and to few-shot 175B GPT-3 **71 ± 4%**; the **1.3B InstructGPT is preferred over the 175B GPT-3** despite ~100x fewer parameters. Truthfulness on TruthfulQA roughly doubles vs GPT-3; closed-domain hallucination drops from **41% (GPT-3) to 21% (InstructGPT)**. With a "respectful" prompt InstructGPT produces ~25% fewer toxic outputs than GPT-3 (Perspective API), but shows no bias improvement on Winogender/CrowS-Pairs. Inter-annotator agreement was 72.6 ± 1.5% (training labelers), 77.3 ± 1.3% (held-out). On cost: the 175B SFT model took **4.9 petaflops/s-days** and the 175B PPO-ptx model **60 petaflops/s-days**, vs **3,640** for pretraining GPT-3 — alignment is a small fraction of pretraining compute.

**Takeaway.** Aligning to human preference via SFT→RM→PPO buys more user-preferred behavior than a 100x parameter increase, at a tiny fraction of pretraining cost — and the alignment tax is largely recoverable by mixing in pretraining gradients (PPO-ptx).
