# Management as AI superpower

**Blog** · Ethan Mollick (One Useful Thing) · 2026-01-27 · [link](https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/management-as-ai-superpower)

**The take (attributed):** Mollick argues that thriving with agentic AI is, fundamentally, a **management problem** — "delegation is the new prompting" — and that the "soft" management skills (scoping, delegating, evaluating) "turned out to be the hard ones."

**Stake:** Wharton/UPenn professor of entrepreneurship; Co-Intelligence author. A business-school scholar naturally framing the agent shift through his own discipline (management/delegation). Weight the delegation framework and GDPval math; note the optimistic adoption framing.

## Argument
- Setup: a 4-day class where non-coding exec MBAs built startups with [[coding-agents|Claude Code]] + Antigravity + chatbots — "an order of magnitude further along… than a full semester before AI." They succeeded by being good at **telling the AI what they want**.
- **The Equation of Agentic Work** — delegate-to-AI depends on three variables: **Human Baseline Time** (how long it'd take you), **Probability of Success** (AI meets your bar per attempt), **AI Process Time** (request + wait + evaluate). Trade "doing the whole task" against "paying the overhead," possibly repeatedly; higher Probability of Success → fewer overhead payments → more worth delegating.
- Grounds it in **GDPval**: ~7-hr Human Baseline Time, ~1-hr expert evaluation, and with **GPT-5.2** the Thinking/Pro models "tied or beat human experts an average of 72% of the time" (vs humans winning most when GDPval first launched). Math: ~3 hours saved on a 7-hour task.
- **Delegation as the new prompting** — three levers (better instructions, better evaluation/feedback, faster quality assessment), all amplified by subject-matter expertise.
- Argues existing delegation paperwork works as AI prompts: **Product Requirements Documents**, shot lists, design intent docs, the Marines' **Five Paragraph Orders**, consulting scopes — all "attempts to get what's in one person's head into someone else's actions." Good delegation specifies: goal & why, limits of authority, definition of done, specific + interim outputs, what to check before claiming finished.
- **Managing agents:** lab devs report their jobs shifting "from mostly programming to mostly management of AI agents"; coding matured first but "isn't the last." Working with agents = **management 101**. The twist: management assumed scarce talent — now "the 'talent' is abundant and cheap. What's scarce is knowing what to ask for."

## Why it matters / where it cuts
The single best articulation of the adoption-layer thesis for a leader: as agents get capable, **org/managerial skill becomes the binding constraint**, not the model. Directly feeds [[agent-adoption]] and rhymes with Lambert's "agents push humans up the org chart" ([[2026-01-21-blog-lambert-get-good-at-agents]]) — the practice-side complement to that researcher's view. The delegation-documentation framing is concretely actionable for deploying agents at work.
