# Jack Clark

*Anthropic's co-founder and Head of Policy, author of the field-shaping **Import AI** newsletter — the radar's clearest insider voice for the frontier-lab "powerful-and-dangerous" worldview and the recursive-self-improvement thesis. Read his safety framing at a discount, his benchmark evidence on its merits.*

## Who they are
Co-founder and **Head of Policy at [[anthropic|Anthropic]]**; before that, policy director at [[openai|OpenAI]]; before AI, a technology journalist. He writes **Import AI**, a weekly newsletter read across the ML community (~70k+ subscribers). Import AI is a **hybrid**: most issues are paper-roundup digests (which belong to the radar's news/research layers), but an occasional **"Import A-Idea" longer-form essay** carries his own opinion — those essays are his perspective-layer contribution and what the radar captures here. He is also a prominent voice in AI policy circles (testimony, frontier-safety frameworks, the "appropriate fear" framing of AI risk).

**Stake:** Anthropic co-founder and the company's chief policy voice. His essays are the most articulate statement of Anthropic's worldview: AI is "grown not made," powerful and somewhat unpredictable, and the state should build transparency/disclosure regimes proactively. This framing **advances Anthropic's regulatory positioning**, and several of his strongest datapoints (model training-speedup numbers, the automated-alignment proof-of-concept) are Anthropic's own. Discount the threat-level rhetoric and the takeoff timelines; weight the publicly-checkable benchmark trends and the policy-process arguments (listening, forced transparency) more heavily. **Flag:** his safety/policy positioning is institutional, not neutral — he is the opposite pole from open-source advocates like [[nathan-lambert]].

## What they argue (recurring stances)
- **"Real and mysterious creatures, not simple machines."** Frontier AI is grown, not made — and increasingly shows situational awareness and complicated goals. The winning move is to "see it for what it is": be a genuine technology optimist (AGI is buildable and near) *while* holding "appropriate fear." ([[2025-10-13-blog-clark-technological-optimism-appropriate-fear|Technological Optimism and Appropriate Fear]].)
- **Recursive self-improvement is close and underpriced.** ~60% chance a frontier model autonomously trains its successor by end of 2028 (~30% by 2027) — argued from public benchmark curves (SWE-Bench, METR time-horizons, CORE-Bench, MLE-Bench, PostTrainBench) rather than vibes. AI can already automate "vast swathes" of AI *engineering*; the creative part of AI *research* is the open question. ([[2026-05-04-blog-clark-automating-ai-research|Automating AI Research]].)
- **Alignment must be near-perfect under recursion.** Compounding error: a 99.9%-accurate alignment technique degrades to ~60% after 500 self-improvement generations — so today's techniques may break exactly when systems get smart enough to matter.
- **A "machine economy" will grow inside the human economy.** Capital-heavy / labor-light AI-run firms, eventually fully autonomous corporations — raising compute-allocation, inequality, and governance questions that markets won't solve on their own.
- **Listen, then legislate; build transparency before the crisis.** Spend less effort convincing the public of specific failure modes and more listening (labor, social, religious groups); a public crisis gives policymakers air cover, so build a pre-existing forced-disclosure regime (employment data, child-safety monitoring, misalignment details) now.

**Evolution 2025 → 2026:** Across 2025 his Import A-Idea essays sharpen from timeline-grappling ("What if the timelines are correct?", Mar 2025) and societal-safety framing (the "existential shock" essay, Jan 2025) to the flagship **emotional-and-policy statement** ("Technological Optimism and Appropriate Fear", Oct 2025). Through 2026 he turns **empirical and economic** — agent ecologies ("Into the mist", Feb 2026), the data-driven **recursive-self-improvement case** ("Automating AI Research", May 2026), and the economic-singularity follow-ups (May 2026). The arc: *how I feel about powerful AI* → *here is the public evidence it's arriving* → *here's what it does to the economy and governance*.
