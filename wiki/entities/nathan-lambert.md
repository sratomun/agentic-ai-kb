# Nathan Lambert

*RL post-training researcher who coined [[rlvr|RLVR]] (Tülu 3, 2024) and writes the widely-read Interconnects — the radar's most nuanced, technically-grounded voice on RL, reasoning, open models, and AI governance.*

## Who they are
Post-training/RL researcher; co-coined **[[rlvr|RLVR]]** in the Tülu 3 work and led post-training on the Olmo open models at the **Allen Institute for AI (Ai2)**. He **left Ai2 in June 2026** (per his "Farewell Ai2" post, Jun 2 2026); his own site says he's "currently doing something new" and **does not name a next employer** — so no new affiliation is asserted here. Writes the technical newsletter **Interconnects**, hosts its interview series, and authors the **RLHF Book** (rlhfbook.com). PhD from UC Berkeley (model-based RL / robotics background).

**Stake:** Researcher and open-models / open-source advocate — low commercial stake. He talks his book on *open weights and public RL infrastructure*, and is a consistent **critic of frontier-lab "AI-as-nuclear-weapons" safety framing**. Discount his open-source optimism and his Anthropic criticism accordingly; weight his technical RL reads heavily (it's his domain).

## What they argue (recurring stances)
- **RL is real and scaling, but long-horizon agency is still *scaffolded, not learned* end-to-end.** Deep Research / Claude Code get robust at *individual* tasks via RL; the long-horizon behavior is assembled by prompting and letting the model run longer, "not sparse credit assignment." (See [[2025-06-09-blog-lambert-what-comes-next-rl|What comes next with RL]], [[2025-06-04-blog-lambert-taxonomy-next-gen-reasoners|reasoning taxonomy]].)
- **Reasoning comes from scaled RL, not search.** The o1/[[deepseek|R1]] generation killed process reward models and [[mcts|MCTS]] as the explanation — it's "a language model with new reasoning behaviors coming from a lot of RL training." ([[2025-01-21-blog-lambert-deepseek-r1-recipe|DeepSeek R1 recipe]].)
- **The frontier's edge is organizational, not secret algorithms** — "most of the key ideas are out there, but open labs tend not to have the resources to put them together in the right configuration." Hence the open-vs-closed gap is real but reproducible. ([[2025-10-20-blog-lambert-how-to-scale-rl|How to scale RL]].)
- **The bottleneck is moving from the model to the human.** With coding agents past a threshold, "agents push the humans up the org chart"; "being good at using AI is a better moat than working hard." ([[2026-01-09-blog-lambert-claude-code-hits-different|Claude Code Hits Different]], [[2026-01-21-blog-lambert-get-good-at-agents|Get Good at Agents]].)
- **Open models > closed; risks are exaggerated** — but he warns the open-source camp not to celebrate state intervention, and that heavy-handed, vibes-based governance is the bigger near-term danger. ([[2026-06-14-blog-lambert-agi-era-ai-governance|AGI era of AI governance]].)

**Evolution 2025 → 2026:** Early 2025 he's a *technical explainer* of the RL/reasoning unlock (R1 recipe, taxonomy, scaling laws) — bullish that the recipe is now clear and open will catch up. Through late 2025 he sharpens a *nuance* line: scaled RL ≠ long-horizon autonomy. By 2026 his lens shifts outward — to **practitioner workflow / human-leverage** (the Claude Code posts) and then to **AI governance and geopolitics** (China lab trips, the AGI-era governance piece) as agents start to bite and the state intervenes.
