# Agent Adoption

*How people and organizations actually take up AI agents — the gap between frontier capability and deployed value, and what closes it.*

## What it is
The practice-and-diffusion layer of the radar: not what agents *can* do in a benchmark, but how real teams adopt, delegate to, manage, and get burned by them. It covers the jagged frontier of capability (agents are superhuman at some tasks and incompetent at adjacent ones), the delegation shift (work moving from doing to managing/reviewing), org-level uptake lag, and the risk of "slop at scale." It borders [[agentic-ai]] (the capability), [[agent-evaluation]] (whether the work is any good), and [[coding-agents]] (the first domain where adoption bit).

## Why it matters
For the exec, this is the layer where capability turns into leverage — or doesn't. The recurring finding from the practitioner/adoption voices is that the binding constraint in 2025-26 is *human*: the bottleneck moves up the org chart to delegation, review, and taste, not raw model ability. Adoption lags capability by months-to-years, so the edge is organizational readiness, not waiting for the next model.

## What the evidence shows
**Perspective layer.** [[ethan-mollick]] is the radar's load-bearing adoption voice: he coined the "Jagged Frontier" framing (→ [[2025-04-20-blog-mollick-jagged-agi]]), tracked the chatbot→agent transition (→ [[2025-11-18-blog-mollick-three-years-from-gpt-3-to-gemini]]), and reframed the 2026 skill as *management* — delegating to and supervising agents (→ [[2026-01-27-blog-mollick-management-as-ai-superpower]]). [[nathan-lambert]] reaches the same place from the research side: "agents push the humans up the org chart" (→ [[2026-01-09-blog-lambert-claude-code-hits-different]]). The work-measurement evidence (GDPval, METR long-task curves) is where adoption claims meet [[agent-evaluation]].

*Honest note: this is an opinion/practice hub, not an arxiv-census cluster — weight it as informed field observation, and read the named voices at their stated stake (Mollick is an adoption-optimist with early model access).*
